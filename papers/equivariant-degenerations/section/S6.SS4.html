6.4. Degeneration to a cusp

Recall that a cusp singularity on a plane curve is called ordinary if no line meets it with multiplicity $\geq 4$. Let $U \subset \mathbb{A}^{1}$ be a suitable neighborhood of $0$ with coordinate $t$. As in Section 6.2, we will write $R$ for the coordinate ring of $U$, with valuation $v$ corresponding to $0$, and we abuse notation and use fractional powers of $t$ to indicate base changes $s^{n}=t$.

Throughout this subsection, we assume $$\alpha_{\on{cusp}}: U \to \P \on{Sym}^{d}V^{\vee}$$ is a family of curves defined by a degree $d$ homogeneous form $F(X,Y,Z)$ with coefficients in $R$, obeying:

  1. The curve $C_{0} := \alpha_{\on{cusp}}(0)$ has an ordinary cusp singularity at $[0:0:1]$ meeting the line $Y=0$ to order $3$,
  2. the total space $\mathcal{C}$ of the family is smooth at the cusp point on $C_{0}$.
Lemma 6.11. (A238)

Maintain the setting above, and let $\gamma(t)$ denote the family of matrices $$\begin{pmatrix} t^{-1/3} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & t^{-1/2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ \end{pmatrix}$$ Then the curve $\alpha_{\on{cusp}}^{\gamma}(0)$ is defined by the equation $$\begin{aligned}\lim_{t\to 0}t^{-1}(F(t^{\frac{1}{3}}X,t^{\frac{1}{2}}Y,Z))\end{aligned}$$ and is projectively equivalent to the curve $C_{\on{flex}}[0]: Z^{d-3}(X^3+Y^2Z+Z^3) = 0$. (See Definition 6.1 [FA95].)

Proof. (75C0)

The proof runs exactly parallel to the proof of Lemma 6.4 [AEE2], so we omit it. \begin{comment} We will work with inhomogenous coefficients $x=\frac{X}{Z}$ and $y=\frac{Y}{Z}$. By the assumption on the singularity at $p$ being an ordinary cusp singularity and meeting $\{X=0\}$ to order 3 at $p$, we know the coefficients of $1$, $x$, $y$, $x^2$, $xy$ of $F(x,y)$ all vanish, and we know the coefficients of $y^2$ and $x^3$ are nonzero. Therefore, the coefficients of $F(t^2x,t^3y)$ are all divisible by $t^6$ and equality holds only for the monomials $y^2$ and $x^3$. Since $G$ does not vanish at $p$, the limit is the coefficients of $y^2$ and $x^3$ of $F$ and the constant term of $G$. \end{comment}

Remark 6.12. (2F48)

Notice that the limit endomorphism $\gamma(0)$ has as kernel space the line $Y=0$, i.e. the cuspidal tangent of the cusp of $C_0$. Meanwhile, the $t=0$ limit of $\gamma^{-1}$ has as its image the cusp point $[0:0:1]$.
