2.3. Proofs of Theorems 1.2 [E65B], 1.3 [5AC4], and 1.4 [BBD3]

With the results of Sections 2.1 and 2.2 in hand, we now restate and prove Theorems 1.3 [5AC4] and 1.4 [BBD3], and deduce from them Theorem 1.2 [E65B] (with the assumption of Theorem 1.5 [6BC3] in the case $g=0$).

Theorem 2.7. (3F87)

{1.3 [5AC4]} Let $\Sigma$ be a topological surface of genus $g\geq1$ with $n\geq0$ punctures. If a semisimple representation $\rho:\pi_1\Sigma\to\GL_2(\C)$ of its fundamental group has finite monodromy along every simple loop on $\Sigma$, then the image of $\rho$ is finite.

Theorem 2.8. (3D50)

{1.4 [BBD3]} Let $\Sigma$ be a topological surface of genus zero with $n\geq0$ punctures. If a semisimple representation $\rho:\pi_1\Sigma\to\GL_2(\C)$ has finite restriction to every pair of pants on $\Sigma$, then the image of $\rho$ is finite.

Proof of Theorems 1.3 [5AC4] and 1.4 [BBD3]. (1894)

Suppose $\rho:\pi_1\Sigma\to\GL_2(\C)$ satisfies the conditions of Theorem 1.3 [5AC4] or 1.4 [BBD3]. Since the morphism $$({\textup{pr}},{\det}):\GL_2(\C)\to\PGL_2(\C)\times\GL_1(\C)$$ has finite kernel, it suffices to show that the compositions ${\textup{pr}}\circ\rho:\pi_1\Sigma\to\PGL_2(\C)$ and ${\det}\circ\rho:\pi_1\Sigma\to\GL_1(\C)$ each have finite image. The fact that ${\det}\circ\rho$ has finite image is clear. To show that ${\textup{pr}}\circ\rho$ has finite image, let us first introduce a punctured surface $\Sigma'=\Sigma\setminus p$ for some $p\in\Sigma$. Since the morphism $i:\pi_1\Sigma'\to\pi_1\Sigma$ is surjective, it suffices to show that ${\textup{pr}}\circ\rho\circ i$ has finite image; choosing a semisimple lift $\tilde\rho:\pi_1\Sigma'\to\SL_2(\C)$ of ${\textup{pr}}\circ\rho\circ i$ (which exists since $\pi_1\Sigma'$ is free), it suffices to show that $\tilde\rho$ has finite image. Note that $\tilde\rho$ has finite monodromy along every simple loop on $\Sigma'$.

Thus, it only remains to prove the theorem in the case where the image of $\rho$ lies in $\SL_2(\C)$. We proceed as outlined in Section 1.2. By Lemma 2.1 [1E06], the character $\tr\rho$ of $\rho$ takes values in the ring of algebraic integers (and a fortiori in $\bar\Q$). Since $\rho$ is semisimple, up to $\SL_2(\C)$-conjugation we may thus assume that the image of $\rho$ lies in $\SL_2(\bar\Q)$. Moreover, any conjugate $\rho^\sigma$ of $\rho$ by an element $\sigma\in\Gal(\bar\Q/\Q)$ of the absoluate Galois group of $\Q$, given as $$\rho^\sigma(a)=(\rho(a))^\sigma\quad\text{for all $a\in\pi_1\Sigma$},$$ satisfies the hypotheses of Theorem 1.3 [5AC4] or 1.4 [BBD3], since having finite order is preserved under $\Gal(\bar\Q/\Q)$. It follows by Lemma 2.1 [1E06] together with Lemma 2.5 [2BE0] (for surfaces of positive genus) or Lemma 2.6 [023B] (for surfaces of genus zero) that the eigenvalues of $\rho(a)$ for each $a\in\pi_1\Sigma$ are algebraic integers (being roots of the characteristic polynomial $X^2-(\tr\rho(a))X+1=0$) whose Galois conjugates all have absolute value $1$ in $\C$, and by Kronecker's theorem they must be roots of unity, i.e. $\rho(a)$ has finite order (note that $\rho(a)$ is semisimple by Lemma 2.5 [2BE0] or 2.6 [023B]). Finally, Selberg's lemma states that any finitely generated subgroup of $\GL_r(L)$ for a field $L$ of characteristic zero has a torsion-free finite-index subgroup. Applied to the image of $\rho$, this shows that the image of $\rho$ is therefore finite.

Remark 2.9. (91D3)

As communicated by Hél\`ene Esnault, one could replace the last steps of the above proof by a simpler argument using discreteness and compactness. On the other hand, the proof presented above has some robust features that may be more widely applicable.

Theorem 2.10. (D2BA)

{1.2 [E65B]} The $p$-curvature conjecture is true for rank $2$ vector bundles with connection on a generic curve of genus $g\geq0$ with $n\geq0$ punctures.

Proof. (30B7)

Let $(V,\nabla)$ be a rank two vector bundle with connection on a generic curve of genus $g\geq0$ with $n\geq0$ punctures, such that almost all $p$-curvatures of $(V,\nabla)$ vanish. By [20, Theorem 1.3], every simple loop has finite monodromy. Moreover, in the case $g=0$, the restriction of its monodromy representation (upon specialization) to any pair of pants is finite by Theorem 1.5 [6BC3].

Choose a specialization of the generic curve to a curve over $\C$ with associated Riemann surface $\Sigma$, and consider the monodromy representation $\rho:\pi_1\Sigma\to\GL_2(\C)$ associated to $(V,\nabla)$. Since the $p$-curvature conjecture is known to be true in the case of solvable monodromy (by Andre [1], Bost [2], and D. Chudnovsky–G. Chudnovsky [4]), we may assume that $\rho$ is irreducible (and in particular semisimple). It follows by Theorems 1.3 [5AC4] and 1.4 [BBD3] that the image of $\rho$ is finite, as desired.
