Section 3. Maximal variation for incompressible and non-defective $X$

The goal of this section is to prove Theorem 1.1 [A0D8]. We begin by proving part (1), which is substantially easier.

Proposition 3.1. (B072)

Let $X \subset \P^n$ be a non-degenerate, normal, incompressible projective variety over a field of characteristic zero. Then $\rho_X$ is a finite map.

Proof. (15D0)

Set $L = \O(1)$ and let $W \subset H^0(X, L)$ be the image of $H^0(\P^n, \O(1))$. Let $V \subset W$ be an $(r+1)$-dimensional subspace. Since $X$ is incompressible, the projection map $p_{V,L} \from X \dashrightarrow \P V$ induced by $(L, V)$ is dominant. By Proposition 2.3 [2E59], this implies that $(L, V)$ is properly ramified. Since $V$ was arbitrary, the projection-ramification map $$\rho \from \Gr(r+1, W) \to |K_X + (r+1) H|$$ is regular. Since the Picard rank of a Grassmannian is $1$, a regular map from a Grassmannian is either constant or finite. It is easy to check that $\rho$ is not constant; so it must be finite.

For the proof of part (2) of Theorem 1.1 [A0D8], we proceed inductively by showing that a general $(n-r-1)$-dimensional linear subspace which is incident to $X$ is an isolated point in its fiber under $\rho$. Again, it is more convenient to work with the more abstract set-up of a linear series, allowing for series that are not very ample.

Let $X$ be a proper variety of dimension $r$, and let $(L, W)$ be a linear series on $X$. For an ideal sheaf $I \subset \O_X$ we denote by $W \otimes I$ the subspace of $W$ consisting of the sections that vanish modulo $I$. More precisely, if $K$ is the kernel of the evaluation map $$W \otimes \O_X \to L \otimes \O_X/I,$$ then $W \otimes I = H^0(X, K)$. In particular, for $W = H^0(X, L)$, we have $W \otimes I = H^0(X, L \otimes I)$. For $s \in W \otimes I$, the vanishing locus $v(s)$ refers to the vanishing locus of $s$ as a section of $L$. We set $|W| = \P W^*$, the space of one-dimensional subspaces of $W$, and likewise $|W \otimes I| = \P (W \otimes I)^*$. For a complete linear series, we write $|L|$ for $|W|$. Note that $v(s) = v(\lambda s)$ for a non-zero scalar $\lambda$, so it causes no ambiguity to talk about $v(s)$ for $s \in |W|$.
