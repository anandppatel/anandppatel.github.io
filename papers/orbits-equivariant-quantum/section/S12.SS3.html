12.3. Degeneration of the general orbit

We now answer Problem 12.2 [9EA7], generalizing the relation in the case $r=2$ to arbitrary $r$, via a relation between generalized matrix orbit classes. The following definition will be used in Section 12.3 only.

Definition 12.7. (9D5F)

Given a partition $A_1\sqcup\ldots\sqcup A_k$ of $\{1,\ldots,n\}$, let $M_{A_1\sqcup\cdots\sqcup A_k}$ denote a choice of a $2\times n$ matrix such that two columns are linearly dependent if and only if their indices lie in the same $A_i$. Equivalently, we may define $$M_{A_1 \sqcup \ldots \sqcup A_k}= \tau^{\le 2}(\tau^{\le 1}(\oplus_{j \in A_1} \ast)\oplus\ldots\oplus\tau^{\le 1}(\oplus_{j \in A_k} \ast)).$$

By Theorem 7.1 [4218], the class $\opc[1]{M_{A_1\sqcup\cdots \sqcup A_k}}$ depends only on the partition $A_1\sqcup\cdots \sqcup A_k$ and not on the specific choice of $M_{A_1\sqcup\cdots\sqcup A_k}$.

Proposition 12.8. (9DDF)

We have $$\begin{aligned}\opc[1]{M_{\{1\}\sqcup\cdots\sqcup \{n\}}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n-2}{\opc[1]{M_{\{1,\ldots,i\}\sqcup \{i+1\}\sqcup \{i+2, \ldots,n\}}}}\end{aligned}$$

Proof. (A949)

This follows by Theorem 7.1 [4218] and the subdivision of Schubert rank polytopes in the proof of Theorem 8.2 [367A]. This also follows from an application of [Kap93, Theorem 1.3.6] in the case where interior vertices of our tree form a chain. Not coincidentially, in characteristic zero, this also follows from Section 4.4.

As a corollary of Proposition 12.8 [9DDF], we have

Theorem 12.9. (4FF0)

With the setup of section, if $d=2r+1$, then the degree of $\phi_X$ is $$\begin{aligned}\sum_{a\ge b>1,a+b+1=d}{2n_{a,b,1}}+4n_{d-2,1,1},\end{aligned}$$ where $$n_{a,b,c} = \int_{\mb{G}(1, r)}s^*[\ol{Z_{a,b,c}}]$$ and $Z_{a,b,c} \subset {\rm Sym}^d(\mb{P}(\mc{S}^\vee)) = \mb{P}({\rm Sym}^d\mc{S}^\vee)$ is the closure of the locus of $n$-tuples of points in each fiber supported at $3$ points with multiplicities $a,b,c$.

Remark 12.10. (6193)

The inverse image $s^{-1}(Z_{a, b, c})$ consists exactly of lines that intersect $X$ at three points with multiplicities exactly $a,b,c$. Hence, we may think of $n_{a, b, c}$ as the number of such lines, counted with multiplicity. In characteristic zero, it is possible to give a transversality argument to show that, for a general $X$, we can take $n_{a,b,c}$ to be the honest number of lines, as all lines appear with multiplicity one.

Proof. (1838)

By Lemma 12.4 [B899], the degree of $\phi_X$ is equal to the degree of $s^{*}\Phi_*\opc[1]{M_{\{1\}\sqcup\cdots\sqcup \{d\}}}$, where $$\begin{aligned}\Phi:\mb{P}(\mc{S}^{\vee})^d\to \mb{P}({\rm Sym}^d \mc{S}^{\vee})\end{aligned}$$ is the multiplication map. By Proposition 12.8 [9DDF], we have $$\begin{aligned}s^{*}\Phi_*\opc[1]{M_{\{1\}\sqcup\cdots\sqcup \{d\}}} = \sum_{i=1}^{d-2} s^{*}\Phi_*\opc[1]{M_{\{1,\ldots,i\}\sqcup \{i+1\}\sqcup \{i+2, \ldots,d\}}}.\end{aligned}$$ Now, observe that $\Phi$ maps $\op[1]{M_{\{1,\ldots,i\}\sqcup \{i+1\}\sqcup \{i+2,\ldots,d\}}}$ generically two-to-one onto $\ol{Z_{i,d-i-1, 1}}$ if $i\in\{1,\frac{d-1}{2},d-2\}$ and birationally onto it otherwise. Therefore $ s^{*}\Phi_*\opc[1]{M_{\{1,\ldots,i\}\sqcup \{i+1\}\sqcup \{i+2, \ldots,d\}}}$ is the class of $2n_{i,d-i-1,1}$ points if $i\in\{1,\frac{d-1}{2},d-2\}$ and $n_{i,d-i-1,1}$ points otherwise. Taking the sum from $i=1$ to $d-2$ yields the desired answer.

Remark 12.11. (D99F)

Using the properties of $[M]_\hbar$ in Section 10 and the description of $M_{A_1 \sqcup \ldots \sqcup A_k}$ in terms of truncations and direct sums with $\ast$, one can compute the $n_{a,b,1}$ directly.

\begin{comment} From our point of view, this relation is obtained by taking the orbit $\op[1][n]{M_{gen}}$ of $n$ general points on $\mb{P}^1$ and expressing it in terms of orbits of $n$ points supported on three points with multiplicities $a$,$b$ and 1. By pushing forward these classes under the multiplication map $$\begin{aligned}\mb{P}(\mc{S}^{\vee})^d\to \mb{P}({\rm Sym}^d \mc{S}^{\vee})\end{aligned}$$ this naturally expresses the degree in terms of the $n_{a,b,1}$'s, if we interpret the $n_{a,b,1}$'s as counting lines with multiplicities. Finally, in characteristic zero, it is possible to give a tranversality argument to show that the lines intersecting $X$ with multiplities $a,b,1$ actually all appear in multiplicity 1. Since the underlying ideas for the relation between the degree and the numbers $n_{a,b,1}$'s are clear and proving everything in detail will carry us too far afield, we will not pursue it further here. \end{comment}
