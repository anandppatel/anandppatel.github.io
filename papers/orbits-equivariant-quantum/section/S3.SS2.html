3.2. High level outline

We now give a high level outline of our proofs. A flow chart containing the logical structure of the proofs is given in Figure 1. Input from the literature is shown in dotted boxes, while our contributions are in solid boxes.

Logical structure of proofs

Non-equivariant additivity

In Theorem 5.2 [48AB], we show the non-equivariant additivity of $M \mapsto \opc{M}$. Li's result [Li17] exhibits the non-equivariant class $\opc{M}$ as the sum of monomials in the $H_i$ variables whose exponent sequence lies inside the region $(r+\frac{1}{n})(1,\ldots,1)-(r+1)P_M$, so the result essentially follows by definition.

Equivariant degenerations of orbits

In Theorem 6.6 [F6CF], we show that any flat limit of the form $\op{M(t)}$ as $t \to 0$ has the property that the special fiber is $\bigcup_{i=1}^k \opc{M_i}$ for some matrices $M_i$ such that the rank polytopes subdivide $P_{M_p}$ for $p$ general. In particular, we deduce in Lemma 6.16 [453E] additivity relations of the form $$\opc{M(p)}=\sum_{i=1}^k \opc{M_i}$$ arising from such degenerations. We do this by identifying enough $\op{M_i}$ in the limit so that both sides agree non-equivariantly, and then conclude by the fact that an effective cycle that is non-equivariantly zero is also equivariantly zero. Note at this point we do not know that $\opc{M}$ depends only on the matroid of $M$.

Relations generated by degenerations

Even though Theorem 6.6 [F6CF] provides many relations among the classes $\opc{M}$, it is not clear that these relations induced from degenerations imply additivity of $\opc{M}$. We will show that they in fact do.

We prove in Lemma 7.6 [6299] that for $M$ having a particular type of matroid polytope, which we call a ``cone-like matroid polytope'' (see Definition 7.5 [2AFC]), $\opc{M}$ depends only on the matroid of $M$. Given $M,M'$ with the same cone-like matroid polytope, we do this by creating a flat degeneration with two special fibers $\op{M}$ and $\op{M'}$.

Using degenerations to reduce to a certain class of matrices with ``positive cone-like matroid polytopes'', which satisfy no non-trivial additive relations, is a delicate combinatorial argument which modifies the algorithm from [DF10, Appendix A]. The essential point is that we closely follow a sequence of infinite polytope subdivisions, which when restricted to $\Delta_{d,n}$ yield a sequence of finite polytope subdivisions. This is done by attaching a certain cone to each matrix in our sequence of degenerations, which governs future degenerations based on past ones. We defer the more detailed high level explanation to Section 7.1.

\begin{comment} A subset of all ``cone-like matroid polytopes'' are ones we call ``positive cone-like matroid polytopes'', which we show have the property that there are no non-trivial additive relations amongst them. Thus our strategy to prove additivity will be as follows. Consider a sum $$\sum a_i 1_{P_{M_i}}$$ of $d \times n$ rank $d$ matrices $M_i$, our goal is to show that $\sum a_i \opc{M_i}$ is supported on a positive codimension subset of $\Delta_{d,n}$.

  1. Use relations obtained from degenerations to repeatedly transform $\sum a_i \opc{M_i}$ until all of the $M_i$ have ``positive cone-like matroid polytopes''. As the relations obtained from degeneration are additive, we still have $\sum a_i \opc{M_i}$ is supported on a positive codimension subset of $\Delta_{d,n}$.
  2. Equate the $\opc{M_i}$ with a common positive cone-like matroid polytope.
  3. Conclude that no terms remain as there are no non-trivial additive relations between ``positive cone-like matroid polytopes''.

The algorithm we use is modeled off of the algorithm from [DF10, Appendix A], which is used to show that every additive relation between matroid polytopes follows from relations induced by matroid polytope subdivisions. The complicated aspect of the algorithm is that it actually involves using a sequence of infinite polytope subdivisions to reduce down arbitrary additive relations, which when restricted to $\Delta_{d,n}$ appears just as a sequence of ordinary subdivisions of matroid polytopes.

The difficulties that we face are that we need to keep track of the specific matrices rather than just their matroids, and we can only obtain subdivisions obtained from degenerations involving these specific matrices.

We use only a particular type of degeneration, adding $t$ times general multiples of some columns to some other columns. We parallel the infinite polytope decomposition process by attaching to each matrix $M$ a ``matroidal cone'' $C$ (see SOMETHING IN THE APPENDIX), such that the Minkowski sum $P_M+C$ corresponds to an infinite polytope from the algorithm of [DF10]. To determine the next degeneration, we observe what subdivision was used at the level of infinite polytopes, and choose an appropriate degeneration of $M$ accordingly so that by attaching appropriate cones to the newly produced matrices we obtain the appropriate infinite polytope subdivision.

Only after all of this do we have that the matroid of $M$ determines $\opc{M}$, from the fact that $\opc{M}=\opc{M'}$ with $M,M'$ having the same matroid polytope is trivially an additive relation.

The combinatorial difficulty amounts to showing that we can find such degenerations provided $M$ and $C$ are ``compatible'' in a certain sense, and that this compatibility propogates through the whole algorithm. \end{comment}

Series and parallel connection

We show that parallel and series connection are given by certain convolutions with the classes of the diagonal in $(\mb{P}^r)^3$ and the class of collinear triples of points in $(\mb{P}^r)^3$ respectively in Section 8. Then identical to the standard derivation of the equivariant $3$-point Gromov-witten invariants of $\mb{P}^r$, we are able to deduce Theorem 3.4 [F221].

Series-parallel matroids

To analyze series-parallel matrices, through their recursive definition it suffices to have a good understanding of how the $\opc{M}$ classes behave under series and parallel connections of the underlying matrices.

Through a clever application of partial fraction decomposition combined with Theorem 3.4 [F221], we are able to show that for each series-parallel matroid there is a universal rational function in $2n$ variables such that for any $r$, if we substitute $H_1,\ldots,H_n,F(H_1),\ldots,F(H_n)$ into the variables then we formally recover the equivariant class. Note that as $r$ increases, the polynomial $F(H_i)$ changes with the addition of more $t_i$ variables.

Formula for $\opc{M}$ (Theorem 3.1 [2A79])

Once we have additivity of the classes $M \mapsto \opc{M}$, to prove Theorem 3.1 [2A79] it suffices to prove it for a special class of matrices whose matroid polytopes generate all others through additivity relations. [DF10] used the additive basis of Schubert matroids, which we will analyze in detail later, but we instead use series-parallel matroids, as every Schubert matroid polytope can be subdivided into series-parallel matroid polytopes.

From additivity we are able to deduce the existence of a rational function for any matrix $M$ determining $\opc{M}$ as above. We show in Theorem 8.2 [367A] that this rational function is uniquely determined by its values when substituting $H_1,\ldots,H_n,H_1^{r+1},\ldots,H_n^{r+1}$ (giving the nonequivariant class of $\op{M}$), so it must agree with the rational function produced by Brion's formula for the non-equivariant classes. Theorem 3.1 [2A79] then follows by substituting $F(H_i)$ in place of $H_i^{r+1}$ in the formula, which is done in Section 9.

Properties of $[M]_{\hbar}$ (Theorem 3.5 [48CA])

To prove Theorem 3.5 [48CA], we use an analogous non-equivariant for every $r$ to equivariant strategy as above, and show that non-equivariantly the data of $[M]_{\hbar}$ can be encoded via a sum of monomials inside $(r+\frac{1}{n})(1,1,\ldots,1)-(r+1)I_M$, by slicing the independence polytope according to the hyperplanes $\{\sum x_i=k\}$ with $k \in \mathbb{Z}$, and showing that each slice, homogenized with an extra variable $H_{n+1}$, appears as the rank polytope of $\tau^{\le k+1}(M\oplus \ast)$. Then all of the parts of Theorem 3.5 [48CA] readily follows as shown in Section 10. For example valuativity of $[M]_{\hbar}$ follows from the fact that valuativity can be interpreted in terms of independence polytopes, and $[M\oplus N]_{\hbar}=[M]_{\hbar}\star [N]_{\hbar}$ follows from the fact that $I_{M\oplus N}=I_M \times I_N$.

Convolution with graph closure $\overline{\Gamma_M}$ (Theorem 3.7 [E134])

Finally, Theorem 3.7 [E134] follows from carefully analyzing the wonderful compactification of [Li09], where we have to relate the images of classes under $(\mu_M)_*$, and $(\mu_{\tau^{\le k+1}(M\oplus \ast)})_*$ under various pullbacks and pushforwards. Then valuativity of $[\overline{\Gamma_M}]$ from Theorem 3.2 [F804] follows from the corresponding valuativity of $[M]_{\hbar}$. This is done in Section 11.
