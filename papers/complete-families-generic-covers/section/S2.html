Section 2. The stack of nodal covers of $X$

Definition 2.1. (8FB7)

Let $d \geq 1, g \geq 2$ be a pair of integers.

  1. The stack $\HdgX$ parametrizes degree $d$ finite flat morphisms $$\alpha: C \to X$$ where
    1. $C$ is a connected, at-worst-nodal projective curve of arithmetic genus $g$, and
    2. $\alpha$ is primitive, i.e. it does not factor through a nontrivial étale cover of $X$.
  2. We denote by $\varphi: \HdgX \to \Mgbar$ the forgetful morphism defined by $$[\alpha: C \to X] \mapsto [C].$$
  3. On $\Mgbar$ we let $\lambda \in \Pic (\Mgbar)$ denote the first chern class of the Hodge bundle, and we let $\delta \in \Pic (\Mgbar)$ denote the total boundary divisor – the sum of each irreducible boundary divisor with multiplicity one.
  4. We abuse notation and use the symbols $\lambda, \delta$ for the respective $\varphi$-pullbacks in $\Pic (\HdgX)$.
Remark 2.2. (9883)

When $g =2$, $\HdgX$ is proper, and when $g > 2$ it is not. This is because when $3$ or more branch points collide, the possible domain curves include worse-than-nodal possibilities. And this is not true for collisions of only two branch points.

Proposition 2.3. (F3F4)

Let $d,g \geq 2$.

  1. $\HdgX$ is irreducible and has dimension $2g-2$.
  2. For fixed $g$, as $d$ ranges over all positive integers, the union $$\bigcup_{d > 0} \varphi(\HdgX) \subset \Mgbar$$ is Zariski-dense.
  3. The group scheme $\Aut(X)$ acts on $\HdgX$ via post-composition, and the morphism $\varphi$ is constant on $\Aut(X)$-orbits.
Proof. (9BE4)

The first statement follows from the well-known fact that a cover $\alpha: C \to X$ is determined by the location of its branch points and discrete monodromy data. The second statement follows from observing that $\Hur{d}{g}(X)$ contains the collection of branched covers of $X$ branched over a single point. The domain curves of this collection of covers, as $d \to \infty$ is known to be Zariski dense in $\mathcal{M}_{g}$ – see, for example, [Che11] for the case of $(X,x)$ a square torus. (The argument works the same for any $j$-invariant.) The last statement is obvious.

Corollary 2.4. (9806)

If $D \subset \Mgbar$ is an effective divisor, then there exist infinitely many positive integers $d$ for which the inclusion $$\varphi^{-1}(D) \subset \HdgX$$ is strict.

Proof. (306B)

Immediate from Proposition 2.3 [F3F4] part (2).

Definition 2.5. (D883)

A rank $r$ and degree $k$ vector bundle $V$ on $X$ is regular polystable if $V = V_{1} \oplus \dots \oplus V_{h}$ where $h = \gcd(r,k)$, and where for all $i$:

  1. $\rk V_{i} = r/h$ for all $i$,
  2. $\deg V_{i} = d/h$ for all $i$,
  3. $V_{i}$ is indecomposable for all $i$, i.e. not a direct sum of smaller bundles, and
  4. $\det V_{i} \neq \det V_{j}$ whenever $i \neq j$.

Recall from Atiyah's classification that if $\gcd(r,k) = 1$ then a rank $r$ and degree $k$ indecomposable bundle is uniquely determined by its determinant. The condition of being regular polystable is an open condition in families of vector bundles.

Definition 2.6. (089F)
  1. Define $\Bog(\mathcal{E}) \subset \HdgX$ (respectively $\Bog(\mathcal{F})$) to be the closed substack parametrizing $\alpha: C \to X$ such that $\mathcal{E}_{\alpha}$ (resp. $\mathcal{F}_{\alpha}$) is not regular polystable.
  2. Define $$\St \subset \HdgX$$ to be the open substack parametrizing covers $\alpha: C \to X$ such that both $\mathcal{E}_{\alpha}$ and $\mathcal{F}_{\alpha}$ are regular polystable vector bundles on $X$. In other words, $$\St = \HdgX \setminus (\Bog(\mathcal{E}) \cup \Bog(\mathcal{F})).$$
  3. Define $$\Hurfixed{d}{g}(X) \subset \HdgX$$ to be the closed substack given by the requirement $$\det \mathcal{E}_{\alpha} \simeq \O_{X}((g-1)x).$$
  4. Define $\Hurfixedstable{d}{g}(X)$ to be the intersection $\St \cap \Hurfixed{d}{g}(X)$.
  5. We will denote by $$\alpha_{\univ}: \mathcal{C} \to \HdgX \times X$$ the universal branched covering and we let $\mathcal{E}_{\univ}, \mathcal{F}_{\univ}$ denote the corresponding universal bundles on $\HdgX \times X$.
  6. We let $\epsilon_{1,1}, \epsilon_{2} \in \Pic(\HdgX)$ (respectively $\varphi_{1,1}, \varphi_{2}$) denote the pushforwards of the classes $$c_{1}^{2}(\mathcal{E}_{\univ}), c_{2}(\mathcal{E}_{\univ})$$ (resp. for $\mathcal{F}_{\univ}$) to $\HdgX$.
Remark 2.7. (E34D)
  1. The classes $c_{1}(\mathcal{E}_{\univ})$ and $c_{1}(\mathcal{F}_{\univ})$ are proportional, satisfying $$(d-3)c_{1}(\mathcal{E}_{\univ}) = c_{1}(\mathcal{F}_{\univ}),$$ and therefore so are $\epsilon_{1,1}$ and $\varphi_{1,1}$.
  2. It is possible to prove (using ideas similar to those in [DP15, Section 2.2]) that the three classes $\epsilon_{1,1}, \epsilon_{2}, \varphi_{2}$ are linearly independent.
  3. The vector bundles $\mathcal{N}_{i}$ arising in the Casnati-Ekedahl-Schreyer resolution of $\alpha_{\univ}$ produce divisor classes which are already in the span of $\epsilon_{1,1}, \epsilon_{2}, \varphi_{2}$, by the results in [DP+18].

The divisor classes $\lambda$ and $\delta$ are contained in the $3$-dimensional vector space $\Q \langle \epsilon_{1,1}, \epsilon_{2}, \varphi_{2} \rangle$.

Proposition 2.8. (9D30)

The classes $\lambda$ and $\delta$ are in the vector space $\Q \langle \epsilon_{1,1}, \epsilon_{2}, \varphi_{2} \rangle$. Specifically, we have: $$\begin{aligned}\lambda &= \ch_{2}(\mathcal{E}_{\univ}) \\ \delta &= 13 \ch_{2}(\mathcal{E}_{\univ}) + \ch_{2}(\mathcal{F}_{\univ}) - \ch_{2}(\Sym^{2}\mathcal{E}_{\univ})\end{aligned}$$ where we have suppressed the pushforward from $\HdgX \times X$ to $\HdgX$.

Proof. (07D9)

Similar divisor class calculations have been done elsewhere, for example in the proof of [DP+18, Proposition 2.8]. It is a standard use of the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch theorem – we leave the details to the reader.

Definition 2.9. (C3F1)

Let $Y$ be a stack. We say there exist plenty of complete curves in $Y$ if for all $n \in \N$ there exists a complete curve $B_{i}$ and non-constant morphism $\beta_{i}:B_{i} \to Y$ such that the morphism $\coprod \beta_{n}: \coprod_{n}B_{n} \to Y$ does not factor through a non-trivial closed substack.

We pose our main question in the form of a conjecture.

Conjecture 2.10. (2541)

There are plenty of complete curves in $\Hurfixedstable{d}{g}(X)$ for any $d,g \geq 2$.
