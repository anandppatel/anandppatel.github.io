2.4. Good families

Our strategy is to compute $[\Orb(X)]$ for a generic $X$ by pulling it back to various families $B \to \mathscr M$. If there is a point of $B$ that lies in every $\Orb(X)$, then we have the issue of excess intersection. A good family is one that does not suffer from this defect. Recall that $V$ is a $4$-dimensional $\k$-vector space.

Definition 2.4. (5026)

A family of cubic surfaces $\pi \colon \cX \to B$ is good if the following holds: there exists a non-empty Zariski open subset $U \subset \Sym^3V^\vee$ such that for every $F \in U$ and $b \in B$, the fiber $\cX_b$ does not lie in $\partial \Orb(F)$.

Example 2.5. (6A03)

Suppose every $\cX_b$ has a finite automorphism group. Then $\pi \colon \cX \to B$ is good. Indeed, let $U$ be the subset consisting of elements with finite automorphism group. For every $F \in U$, the boundary $\partial \Orb(F)$ consists of elements with a positive dimensional automorphism group, and hence it cannot contain $\cX_b$.

Example 2.6. (2D33)

Suppose every fiber $\cX_b$ is GIT-semistable. Then the family $\pi \colon \cX \to B$ is good. Indeed, let $U$ be the subset consisting of GIT-stable elements. For every $F \in U$, the orbit of $F$ is closed in the semistable locus, and hence $\partial \Orb(F)$ cannot contain $\cX_b$.

Example 2.7. (E6E6)

Suppose every fiber $\cX_b$ has a finite automorphism group or is GIT semistable. Let $U$ be the subset corresponding to smooth cubic surfaces. Since smooth cubic surfaces have a finite automorphism group and are GIT stable, we see that $\pi \colon \mathcal X \to B$ is good.

Proposition 2.8. (E2B1)

Let $\pi \colon \cX \to B$ be a good family of cubic surfaces. Assume that $B$ is proper of dimension 4 and a general fiber $\cX_b$ is semistable. Let $\mu \colon B \to \mathscr M$ be the map induced by $\pi$ and $m \colon B \dashrightarrow \mathcal M$ the rational map to the coarse moduli space. Then $$\deg m = \deg \mu^*\left( \Orb(F) \right)$$ for a general cubic form $F$.

Proof. (0C55)

There exists an open $W \subset \mathcal M$ such that $m^{-1} (W) \to W$ is a finite étale morphism. Then the degree of $m$ is simply the number of points in $m^{-1}(u)$ for $u \in W$. By shrinking $W$ if necessary, we may assume that the map $$\mathscr M^{ss} \to \mathcal M$$ is an isomorphism over $W$.

Let $U$ be as in the definition a good family. Then, for every $u \in U$, we have the equality $$\cX \times_{\mathscr M} u = \cX \times_{\mathscr M} \overline u.$$ By shrinking $U$ if necessary, we may assume that $U$ consists of semistable points and maps to $W \subset \mathcal M$ in the coarse space. Then, for every $u \in U$, we have $$\begin{aligned}\deg \mu^* \Orb(u) &= \# \left(\cX \times_{\mathscr M} u \right)\\ &= \# \left(\cX \times_{\mathcal M} u\right) \\ &= \deg m.\end{aligned}$$
