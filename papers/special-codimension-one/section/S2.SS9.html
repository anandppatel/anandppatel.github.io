2.9. Partial pencil families.

This section introduces the class of one parameter families central to the rest of the paper. We call these families partial pencil families, and they lie entirely within the boundary of $\Hdg$ (or $\Hdg^{\rm{adm}}$).

Remark 2.5. (E3DC)

To aid the reader, we mention that our one parameter families will always begin as families in $\Hdg^{\rm{adm}}$, and then if necessary will be lifted to $\Hdg$. This is the main purpose of Lemma 2.4 [E26A].

First we give an informal description of a partial pencil. Consider a boundary component $\Delta \subset \Hdg^{\rm{adm}}$. The general admissible cover parametrized by $\Delta$ can be written as $$\alpha \from C_{L} \cup C_{R} \to P_{L} \cup P_{R}$$ where $\alpha_{L} \from C_{L} \to P_{L}$ and $\alpha_{R} \from C_{R} \to P_{R}$ are the left and right sides of the admissible cover. The essential idea behind the partial pencil families is to vary one of the maps, which we typically take to be the right hand side $\alpha_{R}$, while keeping the left map $\alpha_{L}$ (including the points of attachment) fixed in moduli.

Remark 2.6. (5DE0)

The reader may find it useful to think of partial pencils as the $\Hdg$ or $\Hdg^{\rm{adm}}$ analogues of the standard one parameter families of curves found in the boundary of $\Mg$. One major difference is that in the case of $\Mg$ there exist, conveniently, complete one parameter families lying entirely within a single boundary component while avoiding all others. Unfortunately, partial pencil families typically intersect other boundary components; the management of their intersection with other boundary components is the ultimate technical obstacle in this paper.

Now we give a formal description. Fix a partition $\overline{r} = r_{1} + r_{2} + ... + r_{k} = d$ by positive integers $r_{i}$. Suppose

is a one parameter family of $\overline{r}$-marked admissible covers parameterized by a smooth, proper curve $S$. This means \begin{enumerate} \item $\alpha_{R}$ is a finite map from the nodal family of genus $g_{R}$ curves $\cC_{R}$ to the nodal family of genus $0$ curves $\cP_{R}$.

\item There is a marked section $0 \subset \cP_{R}$, disjoint from the singular locus of the map $p_{R}$, such that $$\alpha^{-1}(0) = r_{1}R_{1} \sqcup r_{2}R_{2} \sqcup ... \sqcup r_{k}R_{k}$$ where $R_{i} \from S \to \cC_{R}$ are sections disjoint from ${\rm Sing}(f_{R})$. In other words, the family (2.1) is ramified over $0$ according to the partition given by $\overline{r}$.

\item Away from $0$ and the singular locus ${\rm Sing}(p_{R})$, the map $\alpha_{R}$ is simply-branched.

\item Above ${\rm Sing}(p_{R})$, $\alpha_{R}$ must satisfy the usual admissible cover ``kissing'' condition. \end{enumerate} (Note: An $\overline{r}$-marked admissible cover is very similar to an admissible cover - we simply allow for higher ramification, given by the profile $\overline{r}$, above a specified marked point $0$ in the target.)

Next, fix a single $\overline{r}$-marked cover $\alpha_{L} \from C_{L} \to (P_{L}, 0)$. Let $r_{1}T_{1}+ r_{2}T_{2}+... +r_{k}T_{k}$ be the preimage of $0 \in P_{L}$, and let $\cC_{L} := C_{L} \times S$, and $\cP_{L} := P_{L} \times S$. We let $L_{i} = T_{i} \times S \subset \cC_{L}$ denote the section along which $\alpha_{L}$ is ramified to order $r_{i}$.

Finally, let $\cC$ be the surface obtained by identifying $L_{i}$ with $R_{i}$ for $i = 1, ... ,k$. Similarly, let $\cP$ be the surface obtained by glueing $0 \in \cP_{L}$ with $0 \in \cP_{R}$. In this way, we construct a family of admissible covers $$\alpha \from \cC \to \cP \to S.$$

Definition 2.7. (DBDB)

A partial pencil is any one-parameter family of admissible covers $\alpha \from \cC \to \cP \to S$ as constructed above. We call $\alpha_R \from \cC_{R} \to \cP_{R}$ (resp. $\alpha_{L}$) the right side (resp. left side) of the partial pencil $\alpha$. We call the $R_{i} \subset \cC_{R}$ (resp. $ L_{i} \subset \cC_{L}$) the right gluing sections (resp. left gluing sections).

Notice that the left side of a partial pencil is fixed in moduli. (Including the points of attachment.)

Suppose $S \to \Hdg^{\rm{adm}}$ is a partial pencil family, lying entirely within a boundary divisor $\Delta$. By Lemma 2.4 [E26A], we may lift this family to a family $S' \to \Delta'$ for any $\Delta' \subset \Hdgn$ lying over $\Delta \subset \Hdg^{\rm{adm}}$. Such families $S' \to \Hdgn$ will be called twisted partial pencils. In particular, the coarse space of a twisted partial pencil is a partial pencil.
