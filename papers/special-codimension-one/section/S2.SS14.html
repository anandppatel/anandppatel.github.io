2.14. Independence of the boundary.

At this point, we have all necessary ingredients to prove independence.

Independence of the boundary of $\widetilde{\H}_{d,g}$.

First we need to understand the boundary of the partial compactification $\widetilde{\H}_{d,g}$:

Proposition 2.14. (9930)

$\sfT$, $\sfD$, and the boundary divisors are independent in ${\rm Pic}_{\Q}\,\widetilde{\H}_{d,g}$.

Proof. (DD3A)

Let $\delta_{i,j}$ be the boundary divisor in $\widetilde{\H}_{d,g}$ generically parametrizing covers $$\alpha \from C_{1} \cup C_{2} \to \P^1$$ where $g(C_{1}) = i$ and $\deg \alpha|_{C_{1}} = j$. (Note: $\delta_{i, j} = \delta_{g-i, d-j}$.) Furthermore, let $\delta_{\rm irr}$ denote the boundary divisor generically parameterizing covers $\alpha \from C \to \P^{1}$ where $C$ is irreducible and nodal.

Begin with any relation: $$0 = a\cdot \sfT + b\cdot \sfD + c \cdot \delta_{\rm irr} + \sum c_{i,j} \cdot \delta_{i,j}$$

We use test families to express the coefficients $c_{i,j}$ as linear combinations of $a,b$, and $c$. The divisor classes $\sfT$, $\sfD$, and $\delta_{\rm irr}$ are known to be independent [DP15][Proposition 2.15].

Lemma 2.15. (5940)

The coefficients $c_{i,j}$ are linear combinations of $a,b$, and $c$.

Proof. (1423)

Let $\Gamma_{k}$ be the family of covers obtained by varying a $(1,k)$ curve in a pencil on $\P^1 \times \P^1$ and attaching a fixed general genus $g$, degree $d-k$ cover $D$ of $\P^1$ at a base point of the pencil.

Then the family $\Gamma_{k}$ interacts nontrivially with the divisors $\delta_{0,k}$, $\delta_{0,k-1}$, $\delta_{0,1}$, $\sfT$ and $\sfD$.

In addition to the families $\Gamma_{k}$ consider the following families:

  1. Begin with a general pencil $p$ of hyperelliptic genus $g$ curves on $\F_{g}$ and attach a fixed general degree $d-2$ genus $0$ cover $R \to \P^{1}$ at a base point of the pencil. $B_{1}$ has nontrivial intersection with $\delta_{0,d-2}$, $\sfT$, $\delta_{\rm irr}$, and $\sfD$.
  2. $B_{2}$: Begin with a pencil $p$ of trigonal genus $g$ covers on a Hirzebruch surface, and attach an unchanging degree $d-3$ rational cover $R \to \P^{1}$ at a base point. $B_{2}$ has nontrivial intersection with $\delta_{0,d-3}$, $\sfT$, $\delta_{\rm irr}$, and $\sfD$.

The families $\Gamma_{k}$, $B_{1}$, and $B_{2}$ provide enough relations to conclude Lemma 2.15 [5940] for coefficients of the form $c_{0,k}$.

Now we argue that knowing Lemma 2.15 [5940] for the coefficients $c_{0,k}$ allows one to deduce it for all coefficients $c_{i,j}$. Indeed, suppose $i >0$. Then it must follow that $j > 1$. (The genus $i$ component must map with degree $j > 1$.)

Consider the following family:

  1. Begin with a general pencil $p$ of hyperelliptic curves of genus $i$ on the Hirzebruch surface $\F_{i}$. At one base point, attach an unchanging genus $g-i$ cover $C_{1} \to \P^{1}$ of degree $d-j$. Choose a second base point (which is not the hyperelliptic conjugate to the first), and attach a second unchanging genus $0$ cover $R_{2} \to \P^{1}$ of degree $j-2$. (If $j = 2$, simply omit $R_{2}$.)

The family $B_{3}$ only interacts nontrivially with $\sfT$, $\delta_{\rm irr}$, $\sfD$, $\delta_{i,j}$ and $\delta_{0,j-2}$. The relations obtained from the test families of type $B_{3}$ allow us to finally conclude Lemma 2.15 [5940].

Let's return to the proof of independence. We use the method of test families. Begin with a dependence relation in ${\rm Pic}_{\Q}\, \Hdg$ $$0 = \sum_\Delta c(\Delta) \cdot \Delta$$ Let us rewrite the dependence relation as:

$$a \sfT + b\Delta_{\rm irr} + c\sfD + \sum_{\Delta'\text{\,\, enum. relevant.}} c(\Delta') \cdot \Delta' + \sum_{\Delta}c(\Delta)\cdot \Delta = 0$$ where the first sum is over all enumeratively relevant divisors other than $\sfT, \sfD$, and $\Delta_{\rm irr}$. We will refer to the coefficients $a$, $b$, $c$, and $c(\Delta')$ as the enumeratively relevant coefficients.

The first claim we make is

Proposition 2.16. (D384)

The coefficients $c(\Delta)$ are linear combinations of enumeratively relevant coefficients.

Before proving Proposition 2.16 [D384], we will need a definition.

Definition 2.17. (CC91)

The excess, ${\rm ex}(\Delta)$, of a boundary divisor $\Delta$ is the number $${\rm ex}(\Delta) : = \min \Big\{r(\Delta)+ \sum_{v \,\, \textrm{L-vertex}} g_v ,\,\, r(\Delta)+ \sum_{v \,\,\textrm{R-vertex}} g_v \Big\}.$$

Observe that the divisors $\Delta$ with ${\rm ex}(\Delta) = 0$ are precisely those which are unramified and have only rational components on one side.

Lemma 2.18. (491F)

If ${\rm ex}(\Delta) =0$, then $c(\Delta)$ is a linear combination of the enumeratively relevant coefficients.

Proof. (03C3)

Pick a rational vertex $v \in \Gamma_{\Delta}$ which has degree $d_v \geq 2$. Vary $v$ in a rational partial pencil as in Section 2.13.1. The resulting partial pencil test family $S$ has negative intersection with $\Delta$ and otherwise only interacts with enumeratively relevant boundary divisors. Therefore, we obtain a relation relating $c(\Delta)$ with enumeratively relevant coefficients, which is what we wanted to show.

Finally, we show that the enumeratively relevant coefficients are zero.

Proposition 2.20. (9BB6)

The enumeratively relevant coefficients $c(\Delta')$ are zero.

Proof. (C806)

This proposition follows from Lemma 2.15 [5940], as we now explain. We note that the Picard groups of the moduli stacks $\tw{\H}_{d,g}, \Hdg$ are the same as that of their coarse spaces after tensoring with $\Q$.

The moduli spaces $\Hdg$ and $\tw{\H}_{d,g}$ share a common open subspace. This means there is a rational map of coarse varieties $$G \from \tw{H}_{d,g} \to \overline{H}_{d,g}$$ where $\tw{H}_{d,g}$ and $\overline{H}_{d,g}$ denote coarse spaces. The birational map $G$ is defined away from a codimension two set in $\tw{H}_{d,g}$, and is one-to-one on the level of points.

The boundary divisors in the image of $G$ are precisely the enumeratively relevant divisors. Therefore, any hypothetical nontrivial relation 2.4 would pull back to a nontrivial relation in ${\rm Pic}_{\Q}\,\tw{H}_{d,g}$, which Proposition 2.14 [9930] prohibits. Thus our proposition follows from Proposition 2.14 [9930].
