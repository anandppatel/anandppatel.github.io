3.2.

Recall that the bundle $\dual{\cE}$ for the map $\alpha$ is defined by the formula: $$\alpha_{*}\cO_{\cC} = \cO_{\P} \oplus \dual{\cE}.$$ In what follows, $ch$ and $ch_{k}$ will denote the Chern character and the degree $k$ part of the Chern character, respectively.

Proposition 3.1. (0CC5)

For the family $f \from \cC \to S$ above, we have: $$\begin{aligned}&\lambda = ch_2(\cE) - \frac{c_{1}^{2}(\cE)}{b}& \\ &\kappa = d \cdot ch_{2}\cE - ch_{2}\cF + \left(\frac{1}{2} - \frac{8}{b}\right)c_{1}^{2}\cE& \\ &\delta = (12-d)ch_{2}\cE + ch_{2}\cF -\left(\frac{1}{2} - \frac{4}{b}\right)c_{1}^{2}\cE&\end{aligned}$$ where $b = 2g+2d-2$ is the number of branch points.

Proof. (1BB1)

First we recall that the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch formula yields: $$\lambda = \chi (\cO_{\cC}) - \chi(\cO_{S})(1-g).$$ Since $\chi(\cO_{\cC}) = \chi(\alpha_{*}\cO_{\cC}) = \chi(\cO_{\P}) + \chi(\dual \cE)$ we get: $$\lambda = \chi(\cO_{\P}) + \chi(\dual \cE) - (1-g)\chi(\cO_{S}) .$$

We calculate $\chi(\dual \cE)$ by applying the Hirzebruch-Reimann-Roch formula to the bundle $\dual \cE$. This says that $$\chi(\dual \cE) = [ch(\dual \cE) \cdot Td(T_{\P})]_{2} = ch_{2}(\dual \cE) + c_1(\cE)(K_{\P}/2) + (d-1)\chi(\cO_{\P}).$$

Let $\sigma \in \Pic_{\Q} \P$ denote the rational section class $[-\omega_{p}/2]$, so that $\sigma^{2} = 0$. Since the degree of $\cE$ is $g+d-1$ when restricted to any $\P^{1} \times \{s\} \subset \P$, we may write $c_{1}(\cE) = (g+d-1)\sigma + mf$, where $f$ is the class of a fiber of $p$.

Squaring gives $$m = \frac{c_{1}^{2}\cE}{b}.$$ The equality $K_{\P}/2 = -\sigma - \chi(\cO_{S})\cdot f$ implies $$c_{1}(\cE) \cdot K_{\P}/2 = -m - (g+d-1) \chi(\cO_{S}).$$

Altogether, we obtain $$\lambda = ch_{2}(\dual \cE) - m + d\chi(\cO_{\P}) - d\chi(\cO_{S}) = ch_{2}(\dual \cE) - \frac{c_{1}^{2}\cE}{b},$$ since $\chi(\cO_{\P}) = \chi(\cO_{S})$. This gives (3.3).

Mumford's relation $12 \lambda = \kappa +\delta$ implies we need only compute $\kappa$. The computation of $\kappa$ uses the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch formula applied to the line bundle $\omega_{\alpha}^{\otimes 2}$ and the map $\alpha$ as in the proof of 1.14 [2558]: $$ch(\alpha_{*}\omega_{\alpha}^{\otimes 2})Td(T_{\pi}) = \alpha_{*}(ch(\omega_{\alpha}^{\otimes 2})Td(T_{f})).$$

By using $ch(\alpha_{*}\omega_{\alpha}^{\otimes 2}) = ch(\Sym^{2}\cE) - ch(\cF)$ and taking degree $2$ parts of the above equation, we end up with the equality $$ch_{2}(\Sym^2\cE) - ch_{2}(\cE) - ch_{2}(\cF) = c_{1}^{2}(\omega_{\alpha}).$$

Finally, by using $c_{1}(\omega_{f}) = c_{1}(\omega_{\alpha}) + \alpha_{*}c_{1}(\omega_{\pi})$, one checks that $$\kappa = c_{1}^{2}(\omega_{f}) = c_{1}^{2}(\omega_{\alpha}) - \frac{8}{b}c_{1}^{2}(\cE).$$

Putting all of the above together yields 3.3.

Let $\sfT \subset S$ and $\sfD \subset S$ denote the divisors of points in $S$ where the map $\alpha_{s} \from C_{s} \to \P^1$ has a triple ramification point and a $(2,2)$ pair of ``doubled" ramification points, respectively.

Proposition 3.2. (DF3E)

We have the following equalities: $$\begin{aligned}&\sfT = (3d-12)ch_{2}\cE - 3ch_{2}\cF + \frac{3}{2}c_{1}^{2}\cE,&\\ &\sfD = 4ch_{2}\cF - (4d-12)ch_{2}\cE.&\end{aligned}$$

Proof. (EDAA)

Let $$\xymatrix @C -1.1pc { \cC \ar[rr]^{\alpha} \ar[rd]_f&& \P \ar[ld]^{\pi} \\ & S}$$ be a general family of covers in $\widetilde{\H}_{d,g}$ with $S$ being a complete curve. We pick the family general enough so that the ramification divisor $R \subset \cC$ of the finite map $\alpha$ is smooth and maps birationally onto the branch divisor $B \subset \P$, with $B$ having simple nodes and cusps only. The cusps of $B$ will then count as intersections of our family with the divisor $\sfT$ and the nodes of $B$ will count as intersections with the divisor $\sfD$. We then see that $$p_{a}(B) - p_{a}(R) = \sfT + \sfD$$ By adjunction on $\cC$ and $\P$, this leads to the relation $$B^{2} + c_{1}\omega_{\pi}\cdot B - (R^{2} + c_{1}\omega_{f}\cdot R) = B^{2} - 2R^{2} = \sfT + \sfD$$

On the other hand, the ramification divisor $R$ is itself a branched cover of $S$ under the map $f$. The branch points of the map $f|_{R} \from R \to S$ provide intersections with $\delta$ or with $\sfT$. By adjunction on the surface $\cC$, we get the equality $$R^{2} + R \cdot c_{1}\omega_{f} = 2R^{2} + B \cdot c_{1}\omega_{\pi}= \sfT + \delta$$

Recall that $B = 2c_{1}\cE$, so $B^{2} = 4c_{1}^{2}\cE$. Furthermore, from the proof of Proposition 3.1 [0CC5] we see that $B \cdot \omega_{\pi} = \frac{-2c_{1}^{2}\cE}{b}$. The current proposition now follows from Proposition 3.1 [0CC5] and the following lemma:

Lemma 3.3. (6831)

We have $$R^{2} = d \cdot ch_{2}\cE - ch_{2}\cF + \frac{c_{1}^{2}\cE}{2}.$$

Proof of Lemma 3.3 [6831]. (D528)

This follows from applying the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch formula to the map $\alpha \from \cC \to \P$ and the sheaf $\cO_{\cC}(2R) = \omega_{\alpha}^{\otimes 2}$.

Proposition 3.4. (78BF)

For a family $f \from \cC \to S$ as above, $$24(b-1)\lambda - 3(b-2)\delta + 6\sfD - (b-10)\sfT = 0$$ where $b = 2g+2d-2$ is the number of branch points.

Proof. (EDB3)

This follows immediately from Proposition 3.1 [0CC5] and Proposition 3.2 [DF3E].
