3.11. Constructing families of tetragonal curves.

For any branched cover $[\alpha \from C \to \P^{1}] \in \td{\H}_{4,g}$, the Casnati-Ekedahl resolution is determined by the map $$\pi^{*}\cF(-2) \to \cO_{\P \cE}$$ which in turn is determined, up to scalar multiple, by a global section $\eta \in H^{0}(\P^{1}, \dual{\cF}\otimes \Sym^{2}\cE)$. For a general tetragonal curve $C$, the bundles $\cF$ and $\cE$ are balanced, and we assume they are throughout this section.

The locus $Z \subset \P(H^{0}(\P^{1}, \dual{\cF}\otimes \Sym^{2}\cE))$ parametrizing worse-than-nodal schemes in $\P \cE$ is has codimension two, as seen by a straightforward parameter count and Bertini's theorem.

Constructing families of tetragonal curves I.

Take a general linear pencil $\P^{1}_{t} \subset \P(H^{0}(\P^{1}, \dual{\cF}\otimes \Sym^{2}\cE))$, and consider the resulting family $f \from \cC \to \P^{1}_{t}.$ We have the exact analogue of Proposition 3.14 [E6F1]:

Proposition 3.16. (12E7)

Let $[\P^{1}_{t}] \in A_{1}(\td{\H}_{4,g} \setminus \sfM \cup \sfCE)$ be the curve class given by a pencil $\P^{1}_{t} \subset \P(H^{0}(\P^{1}, \dual{\cF}\otimes \Sym^{2}\cE))$ as above. Then there exists a positive integer $m$ with the following property: There exists an irreducible proper curve of class $m[\P^{1}_{t}]$ contained in $\td{\H}_{4,g} \setminus (\sfM \cup \sfCE)$ which passes through any two prescribed points $\alpha_{1}, \alpha_{2} \in \td{\H}_{4,g} \setminus (\sfM \cup \sfCE)$.

Proof. (69AC)

The proof is the same as the proof of Proposition 3.14 [E6F1].

Theorem 3.17. (949E)

The effective divisors $\sfM$ and $\sfCE$ are rigid and extremal in the effective cone of divisors $\Eff (\overline{\H}^{(2)}_{4,g})$ with the exception of $\H_{4,3}$ where the divisor $\sfCE$ has two components $\sfA$ and $\sfB$, both of which are extremal and rigid.

Proof. (3476)

We prove the theorem for $\sfM$; the argument for $\sfCE$ is the same, up to the exception. Suppose we could write $$\sfM = \sum_{i} a_{i}E_{i}$$ where $E_{i} \subset \overline{\H}^{(2)}_{4,g}$ are irreducible effective divisors, none supported on $\sfM$ and $a_{i} > 0$.

The curve class $m[\P^{1}_{t}]$ clearly has zero intersection with $\sfM$, and therefore must have zero intersection with each irreducible divisor $E_{i}$. Proposition 3.16 [12E7] then implies that the divisors $E_{i}$ must be supported possibly on $\sfCE$ or the higher boundary divisors. The same proposition also implies that $\sfM$ is rigid: a deformation $M'$ would have to intersect the class $m[\P^{1}]$ nontrivially.

We conclude that the relation $\sfM = \sum a_{i}E_{i}$ then gives a linear dependence among the boundary divisors of $\overline{\H}^{(2)}_{4,g}$ and $\sfCE$. This is impossible by Corollary 3.6 [5EAD].

We now address the exception. The central observation is that the Casnati-Ekedahl divisor $\sfCE$ is reducibleprecisely when $g=3$. In fact, when $g=3$, the Casnati-Ekedahl divisor $\sfCE$ contains $\sfM$ as an irreducible component, and has one residual component.

The irreducibility of the Casnati-Ekedahl divisor $\sfCE$ in all other cases is proved in [DP15][Proposition 4.7] and the reducible example is discussed in [DP15][Example 4.8].

With this caution, the rest of the argument for $\sfCE$ goes through as in the above argument for $\sfM$.

Constructing families of tetragonal curves II.

The Casnati-Ekedahl resolution 3.14 reflects a classical fact about tetragonal curves: A genus $g_R$ cover $[\alpha \colon C \longrightarrow P^1]$ rests in its relative canonical embedding $\pi \colon P\mathcal{\cE} \longrightarrow P^1$ as a complete intersection of two relative conic divisors $Q_u$ and $Q_v$ in the linear systems $|2\zeta -uf|$ and $|2\zeta - vf|$, respectively. Here $\zeta$ and $f$ denote the hyperplane and fiber classes of $\P \cE$.

The integers $u$ and $v$ are such that $\cF_{\alpha} = \cO(u)\oplus \cO(v)$. Moreover by Proposition 1.14 [2558], $u+v = g_{R}+3.$ We assume that $u\leq v$, so that generically in $\H_{4,g_{R}}$, $v = \lceil{\frac{g_R+3}{2}\rceil}$. For a general cover $\alpha$, the line bundle $\cO_{\P\cE}(2\zeta - vf)$ is globally generated on $\bf{P}\cE$, and $\cO_{\P\cE}(2\zeta - uf)$ is very ample. We assume from here on that the bundles $\cE$ and $\cF$ are balanced.

Fix a smooth surface $\F \in |2\zeta - vf|$, and consider a general pencil $P^1_t \subset |2\zeta-uf|$ on $\F$. Let $p \colon Y \longrightarrow P^1_t$ denote the total space of the pencil. So $p$ is a family of tetragonal curves, and $Y$ is the blow up of $\F$ at the base locus of the pencil. We compute the invariants $\delta \cdot p$ and $\lambda \cdot p$ using standard methods.

Proposition 3.18. (2CF9)

For a general pencil $p \colon Y \longrightarrow P^1_t$ of the type described above, $$\begin{aligned}\lambda \cdot p &=& g_R,\\ \delta \cdot p &=& v + 6g_R + 6.\\\end{aligned}$$ Furthermore, every singular fiber of $p$ is simply nodal.

Proof. (D9F0)

The last statement is a straightforward application of Bertini's theorem.

Since $\chi(\cO_{\sfY}) = 1$ ($Y$ is rational), it follows that $$\lambda \cdot p = \chi(\cO_{\sfY}) - \chi(\cO_{P^1_t})\chi(\cO_{C_{\gen}}) = g_R,$$ where $C_{\gen}$ denotes the curve in the pencil.

We now compute $\delta \cdot p$. We recall the following fact, easily seen using jet bundles: If $L$ is an ample line bundle on a surface $S$, then a general pencil in $|L|$ has $c_{2(L + L \otimes \Omega_{S})$ singular elements.}

Letting $L := \cO_\F (2\zeta-uf)$, get: $$\begin{aligned}\delta \cdot p &=& c_2(L + L \otimes \Omega_\F) = c_1(L) \cdot c_1(L \otimes \Omega_\F)+ c_2(L \otimes \Omega_\F)\\ &=&c_1(L) [K_\F + 2c_1(L)] + c_2(\Omega_\F) + c_1(L) K_\F + c_1^2(L)\\ &=& 3c_1^2(L) + 2c_1(L) \cdot K_\F + c_2(\Omega_{\F})\\ &=& 2(2g_R-2) + c_1^2(L) + c_2(\Omega_{\F}). \hspace{1 in}\end{aligned}$$

We compute $c_{1}^{2}(L)$: $$\begin{aligned}c_1^2(L) &=& (2\zeta - uf)^2(2\zeta-vf) = [4\zeta^2 - 4u\zeta f][2\zeta - vf]\\ &=& 8\zeta^3 - 8u - 4v = 8(u+v)-8u-4v = 4v,\\\end{aligned}$$ which means $$\begin{aligned}\delta \cdot p &=& 2(2g_R-2) +4v + c_2(\Omega_{\F}).\end{aligned}$$ So it suffices to compute $c_2(\Omega_{\F})$. In order to compute this, we use the exact sequence of sheaves on $ P\cE$: $$0 \to \cO_{\F}(-2\zeta +vf) \to \Omega_{P\cE}|_{\F} \to \Omega_{\F} \to 0$$ Put $M := \cO_{\F}(-2\zeta +vf)$. Then the Whitney sum formula says: $$\begin{aligned}&&c(\Omega_{\F}) \cdot (1 + c_1(M)t) = c(\Omega_{P\cE}|_{\F})\\\end{aligned}$$ and so we get: $$\begin{aligned}c_2(\Omega_{\F}) = c_1^2(M) - K_{P\cE}|_{\F} \cdot c_1(M) + c_2(\Omega_{P\cE})|_{\F}.\end{aligned}$$

The canonical class of $P\cE$, $K_{P\cE}$, is $-3\zeta + (u+v - 2)f$, so the first part of the right hand expression becomes $$\begin{aligned}-4\zeta^3 + 4u +4v - 8 = -8 .\end{aligned}$$ In order to compute $c_2(\Omega_{P\cE})|_{\F}$ , we consider the Euler sequence $$0 \to \Omega_{\pi} \to \pi^{*}\cE(-1) \to \cO \to 0$$ and the relative cotangent sequence $$0 \to \cO(-2f) \to \Omega_{P\cE} \to \Omega_{\pi} \to 0.$$ We find that: $$\begin{aligned}c_2(\Omega_{P\cE})|_{\F} = 3v + 6u - 4g_R.\end{aligned}$$ and $$c_2(\Omega_{\F}) = 3v + 6u - 4g_R - 8.$$

The proposition now follows by putting these calculations together.
