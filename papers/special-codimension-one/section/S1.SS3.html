1.3.

The bundles $\cE$ and $\cF$ are two bundles among a whole host of bundles naturally associated to a branched cover, as explained by a theorem of Casnati and Ekedahl. We review this fundamental theorem here.

Let $X$ and $Y$ be integral schemes and $\alpha \from X \to Y$ a finite flat Gorenstein morphism of degree $d \geq 3$. The map $\alpha$ gives an exact sequence $$0 \to \cO_Y \to \alpha_* \cO_X \to {\cE_\alpha}^\vee \to 0,$$ where $\cE = \cE_\alpha$ is a vector bundle of rank $(d-1)$ on $Y$, called the Tschirnhausen bundle of $\alpha$. Denote by $\omega_\alpha$ the dualizing sheaf of $\alpha$. Applying $Hom_Y(-,\cO_Y)$ to (1.3), we get $$0 \to \cE \to \alpha_* \omega_\alpha \to \cO_Y \to 0.$$ The map $\cE \to \alpha_* \omega_\alpha$ induces a map $\alpha^* \cE \to \omega_\alpha$.

Theorem 1.6. (6A2D)

[CE96, Theorem 2.1] In the setting above, $\alpha^* \cE \to \omega_\alpha$ gives an embedding $\iota \from X \to \P \cE$ with $\alpha = \pi \circ \iota$, where $\pi \from \P \cE \to Y$ is the projection. Moreover, the subscheme $X \subset \P \cE$ can be described as follows.

  1. The resolution of $\cO_X$ as an $\cO_{\P \cE}$-module has the form $$\begin{split} 0 \to \pi^* \cN_{d-2} (-d) \to \pi^* \cN_{d-3}(-d+2) \to \pi^*\cN_{d-4}(-d+3) \to \dots \\ \dots \to \pi^*\cN_2(-3) \to \pi^*\cN_1(-2) \to \cO_{\P \cE} \to \cO_X \to 0, \end{split}$$ where $\cN_i$ are vector bundles on $Y$. Restricted to a point $y \in Y$, this sequence is the minimal free resolution of length $d$, zero dimensional scheme $X_y \subset \P \cE_y\cong \P^{d-2}$.
  2. The ranks of the $\cN_i$ are given by $$\rk \cN_i = \frac{i(d-2-i)}{d-1} {d \choose {i+1}},$$
  3. We have $\cN_{d-2} \cong \pi^* \det \cE$. Furthermore, the resolution is symmetric, that is, isomorphic to the resolution obtained by applying $Hom_{\cO_{\P \cE}}(-, \cN_{d-2}(-d))$.

The branch divisor of $\alpha \from X \to Y$ is given by a section of $(\det \cE)^{\otimes 2}$. In particular, if $X$ is a curve of (arithmetic) genus $g$, $\alpha$ has degree $d$, and $Y = \P^1$, then $$\rk \cE = d-1 \,\, \text{and } \deg \cE = g+d-1.$$
