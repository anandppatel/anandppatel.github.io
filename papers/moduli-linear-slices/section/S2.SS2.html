2.2. Lines meeting $X$ infrequently

\begin{notation} Let $\sum_{i}^{k}m_{i} = d$, $\sum_{i=1}^{j}n_{i}=d$, and let $\P^{N}$ denote the projective space of degree $d$ hypersurfaces in $\P^{r}$. We define the following three types of incidence correspondences: \begin{enumerate} \item $$\begin{aligned}I_{k} & \left( m_{1}, \dots, m_{k} \right) =\\ & \overline{\left\{ (X,\ell) \mid X \cap \ell = \sum_{i}m_{i}p_{i}, \, \, \text{for distinct $p_{i} \in \ell$} \right\}} \subset \P^{N} \times \G\left( 1,r \right)\end{aligned}$$ \item $$\begin{aligned}I'_{k} & \left( m_{1}, \dots, m_{k} \right) =\\ & \overline{\left\{ (X,\ell,p_{1}, \dots, p_{k}) \mid X \cap \ell = \sum_{i}m_{i}p_{i} \right\}} \subset \P^{N} \times \P \times_{\G\left( 1,r \right)} \dots \times_{\G\left( 1,r \right)}\P\end{aligned}$$ \item $$\begin{aligned}& I_{k,j}'' \left( m_{1},\dots, m_{k} \mid n_{1}, \dots, n_{j}\right) =\\ & \overline{\left\{ (X, \ell, p_{1}, \dots, p_{k}, \ell', q_{1}, \dots, q_{j}) \mid X\cap \ell = \sum_{i}m_{i}p_{i}, \, \, \, X \cap \ell' = \sum_{i}n_{i}q_{i} \right\}}\end{aligned}$$

\end{enumerate}

The closures are taken of the sets where the objects parametrized are all distinct (for points) and do not intersect (for lines).

\end{notation}

Lemmas on correspondences

Lemma 2.11. (852C)

$I_{k}\left( m_{1}, \dots, m_{k} \right)$ is irreducible for all $k$.

Proof. (3D2F)

Consider the correspondence $I_{k}'\left( m_{1}, \dots, m_{k} \right)$. The projection to the data $(\ell, p_{1}, \dots, p_{k})$ makes $I_{k}'$ into a projective space bundle over the parameter space of tuples $\left( \ell, p_{1}, \dots, p_{k} \right)$, and hence is irreducible. $I_{k}'\left( m_{1}, \dots, m_{k} \right)$ dominates $I_{k}\left( m_{1}, \dots, m_{k} \right)$ by forgetting the data of the marked points $(p_{1}, \dots, p_{k})$. The lemma follows.

Lemma 2.12. (408C)

The varieties $I_{k,j}''\left( m_{1}, \dots, m_{k} \mid n_{1},\dots,n_{j} \right)$ are irreducible for all $k, j$, and all multiplicities $(m_{1}, \dots, m_{k}), ( n_{1}, \dots, n_{j} )$.

Proof. (7C8A)

The general fiber of the projection $$\begin{aligned}(X ,\ell, p_{1}, \dots, p_{k}, \ell', q_{1}, \dots, q_{j}) \mapsto (\ell, p_{1}, \dots p_{k}, \ell', q_{1}, \dots, q_{j})\end{aligned}$$ is a linear space in $\P^{N}$ with dimension independent of the pair of non-intersecting lines $\ell, \ell'$. This follows because the restriction map $$\begin{aligned}\Gamma (O_{\P^{r}}(d)) \to \Gamma (O_{\ell \cup \ell'}(d))\end{aligned}$$ is surjective. The lemma follows at once.

Definition 2.13. (9023)

Let $X \subset \P^{r}$ be a hypersuface. A line $\ell$ is $k$-incident to $X$ if the set-theoretic intersection $X\cap \ell$ consists of $k$ points.

Proposition 2.14. (370D)

Assume $k =d - (2r-2)$ is positive. Then a general hypersurface $X \subset \P^{r}$ of degree $d$ has finitely many $k$-incident lines, and has no $k'$-incident lines for $k' < k$.

Proof. (45AA)

This is standard, and left to the reader.

First-order analysis near a $k$-incident line

Let $(X,\ell) \in I_{k}(m_{1},\dots,m_{k})$ be a general point. We will study the first order deformations of $X \cap \ell$, as we vary $\ell$.

Fix homogeneous coordinates $\left[ x_{0}, \dots , x_{r} \right]$ for $\P^{r}$ such that $\ell$ is given by $x_{0} = \dots = x_{r-2} = 0$. Let $X$ be given by the degree $d$ equation $$\begin{aligned}F = P(x_{r-1}, x_{r}) + \sum_{j=0}^{d-2}x_{j}G_{j}\end{aligned}$$ where $$\begin{aligned}P = \prod_{i=1}^{k}\left( x_{r-1} - \alpha_{i}x_{r} \right)^{m_{i}}.\end{aligned}$$

Definition 2.15. (73DA)

Let $G \subset \PGL_{r+1}$ be the group of matrices of the form: $$\begin{aligned}G = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\ \hdotsfor{5} \\ a_{r-1,0} & a_{r-1,1} & \dots & 1 & 0 \\ a_{r,0} & a_{r,1} & \dots & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\end{aligned}$$ where $a_{i,j}$, $(i = r-1, r), (j = 0 , \dots , r-2)$ are any constants.

Remark 2.16. (2127)

The orbit $G \cdot [\ell]$ is an open subset of $\G(1,r)$ isomorphic to $\A^{2r-2}$.

Definition 2.17. (4E5F)

Let $Z \subset \ell \times G$ be the union $$\begin{aligned}\bigcup_{g \in G} \left(\left(g \cdot X\right)\cap \ell,g\right).\end{aligned}$$

Lemma 2.18. (E0FD)

The variety $Z \subset \ell \times G$ is isomorphic to the the variety $\Sigma \subset \P$ above the open set $G \subset \G\left( 1,r \right)$.

Proof. (9AB2)

This is clear.

Our goal here is to understand the first-order infinitesimal neighborhood of the the family $Z \subset \ell \times G \to G$ near the point $\left[ \ell \right] \in G$.

Definition 2.19. (A1F5)

We let $g_{j}$ be the polynomial in $x_{r-1},x_{r}$ which is the restriction of $G_{j}$ to $\ell$.

Lemma 2.20. (78AE)

Let $\left\{ c_{i,j} \right\}, (i=r-1,r), (j=0,\dots,r-2)$ be the elements $\partial_{a_{i,j}} \in T_{\left[ \ell \right]}G$.

The first-order family $Z_{\varepsilon} \subset \ell \times T_{\left[ \ell \right]}G \to T_{\left[ \ell \right]}G$ is isomorphic to the zero locus of: $$\begin{aligned}P\left( x_{r-1},x_{r} \right) + \sum_{j=0}^{r-2}\left( c_{r-1,j}x_{r-1}+c_{r,j}x_{r} \right)g_{j}.\end{aligned}$$

Proof. (9ABD)

The first-order family parametrized by the Lie algebra of the group $G$ is given by applying the infinitesimal substitutions $$\begin{aligned}x_{0} &\mapsto x_{0} + \varepsilon(c_{r-1,0}x_{r-1}+ c_{r,0}x_{r})\\ x_{1} &\mapsto x_{1} + \varepsilon(c_{r-1,1}x_{r-1}+c_{r,1}x_{r})\\ &\vdots\\ x_{r-1} &\mapsto x_{r-1}\\ x_{r} &\mapsto x_{r}\end{aligned}$$ to the equation $F$ defining $X$ and then restricting to $\ell$. The constants $\left\{ c_{i,j} \right\}$ vary freely, and represent coordinates on the Lie algebra of $G$. The end result is the family given in the statement of the lemma.

Remark 2.21. (FF7B)

It will usually be more convenient to use the affine notation $$\begin{aligned}p(z) + \sum_{j=0}^{r-2}\left( c_{r-1,j}z+c_{r,j} \right)g_{j}(z).\end{aligned}$$

Using the infinitesimal analysis above, we will now prove some lemmas which will allow us to understand the versality of the point-line correspondence $\Sigma \to \G\left( 1,r \right)$ near a $k$-incident line $\ell$, where $k = d -(2r-2)$.
