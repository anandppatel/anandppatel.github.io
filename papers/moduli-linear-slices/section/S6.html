Section 6. Bounds and computations for $B_k(n)$

We will bound $B_k(n)$ for all $k,n$ in Proposition 6.1 [3F66]. We also compute $B_{k}(n)$ for $k$ small and arbitrary $n$, and give some conjectures about $B_{k}(n)$ in general.

To give an idea of how the function $B_k(n)$ behaves, we note the results in Section 6 can show $B_5(39)=39$, corresponding to the admissible sequence $$(0,1)(1,5)(1,3)(1,2)(2,3)(4,5)(1,1)(6,5)(4,3)(3,2)(5,3)(9,5)(2,1).$$

There are a couple of features of this admissible sequence we believe hold in general that we will only prove in special cases. First, this admissible sequence can be generated greedily, where we use greed to maximize the ratio $\frac{d}{r}$ of the last piece of the sequence. Second, the admissible sequence is essentially periodic in that the $(1,1)(6,5)(4,3)(3,2)(5,3)(9,5)$ is obtained from $(0,1)(1,5)(1,3)(1,2)(2,3)(4,5)$ by replacing each $(d,r)$ with $(d+r,r)$. We give a finite criterion that can be applied to show both the greedy property and the periodicity in Lemma 6.5 [0A59] in Section 6 below.

We expect there are many other interesting patterns that can be found. For example, the segment $(0,1)(1,5)(1,3)(1,2)(2,3)(4,5)(1,1)$ of the admissible sequence above is preserved under reversing the order and replacing each $(d,r)$ with $(r-d,r)$. This pattern continues to hold for larger $k$ and suggests that these optimal admissible sequences can also be generated greedily backwards as well as forwards.

Proposition 6.1. (3F66)

We have $B_k(n) \leq 3 \left(\frac{n^2}{k^{\frac{3}{2}}}+k^{\frac{3}{2}} \right)$

Proof. (4F6D)

Let $(d_1,r_1), \cdots, (d_a,r_a)$ be an admissible sequence with $\sum_i d_i = B_k(n)$. Let $\mu_i = \frac{d_i}{r_i}$. Let $n(j)$ be the sum of the $r_i$ such that $\mu_i\in [j-1,j)$.

Since the $\mu_i$ contributing to $n(j)$ are all less than $j$, we observe that $$B_k(n) \leq \sum_{j=1}^\infty jn(j) .$$ Thus, understanding the $n(j)$ allows us to bound $B_k(n)$. The sum of all of the $n(j)$ is $n$. Let $J$ be the last nonzero $n(j)$, so $B_k(n) \leq \sum_{j=1}^{J} j n(j)$.

Let $n_k^{\min}$ be a positive number that is at most $n(j)$ for any $j < J$. Then we obtain an upper bound for $B_k(n)$ $$\begin{aligned}B_k(n)& \leq \sum_{i=1}^J jn(j)\leq n_k^{\min}+2n_k^{\min}+\cdots+\lceil \frac{n}{n_k^{\min}}\rceil n_k^{\min}\\ &= n_k^{\min} \frac{\lceil \frac{n}{n_k^{\min}}\rceil(1+\lceil\frac{n}{n_k^{\min}}\rceil)}{2}\\ &\leq n_k^{\min} \frac{(\frac{n}{n_k^{\min}}+1)(\frac{n}{n_k^{\min}}+2)}{2}\end{aligned}$$

Thus, it remains to give a bound for $n_k^{\min}$. Fix $j1$, then this is true because $\frac{d_{i_j+1}}{r_{i_j+1}}\leq \frac{d_{i_j}}{r_{i_j}}+\frac{1}{k}<(j-1)+\frac{1}{k}$.

Since $\frac{d_{i_j+1}}{r_{i_j+1}}< (j-1)+\frac{1}{k}$, we then see that $$\begin{aligned}\frac{d_{i_j+2}}{r_{i_j+2}}&\leq \frac{d_{i_j+1}}{r_{i_j+1}}+\frac{1}{k}< (j-1)+\frac{2}{k}\\ \vdots\quad &\quad \vdots\\ \frac{d_{i_j+k}}{r_{i_j+k}}&\leq \frac{d_{i_j+k-1}}{r_{i_j+k-1}}+\frac{1}{k}

Now, we want to bound $r_{i_j+1}+\cdots+r_{i_j+c(j)}=n(j)$. In the multi-set $\{r_{i_j+1},\ldots,r_{i_j+c(j)}\}$, we know that there is at most element that is equal to 1, fewer than two elements that are equal to 2, fewer than three elements that are equal to 3 and so on. Therefore, if $m$ is the largest integer such that $1+(1+\cdots + m-1) = 1+\frac{m(m-1)}{2}$ is at most $k$, then $\frac{(m-1)^2}{2}<\frac{m(m_1)}{2}+1\leq k$, so $m \leq \sqrt{2k}+1$. Thus, $$\begin{aligned}n(j) = r_{i_j+1}+\cdots+r_{i_j+c(j)}&\geq 1+(2\cdot 1+3\cdot 2+\cdots m\cdot(m-1))\\ &= 1+2(\binom{2}{2}+\binom{3}{2}+\cdots+\binom{m}{2})\\ &= 1+ \frac{(m+1)m(m-1)}{3}\\ &\geq \frac{(\sqrt{2k}+2)(\sqrt{2k}+1)\sqrt{2k}}{3}>\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}k^{\frac{3}{2}}.\end{aligned}$$ Thus, choosing $n_k^{\min}$ to be $\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}k^{\frac{3}{2}}$ suffices. Plugging into our earlier bound, we get an upper bound for $B_k(n)$ as $$\begin{aligned}n_k^{\min} \frac{(\frac{n}{n_k^{\min}}+1)(\frac{n}{n_k^{\min}}+2)}{2}=\frac{n^2}{2n_k^{\min}}+ \frac{3n}{2}+n_k^{\min}=\frac{n^2}{k^{\frac{3}{2}}}\frac{9}{4\sqrt{2}}+\frac{3n}{2}+\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}k^{\frac{3}{2}},\end{aligned}$$ which is at most $2(\frac{n^2}{k^{\frac{3}{2}}}+n+k^{\frac{3}{2}})$. Applying the AM-GM inequality yields $$\begin{aligned}\frac{n^2}{k^{\frac{3}{2}}}+k^{\frac{3}{2}}\geq 2n,\end{aligned}$$ yielding the claimed bound.

We now move on to computing exact values of $B_k(n)$ for small $k$. Our strategy is a recursive algorithm that requires some conditions to be met, and we suspect that these conditions are always met. In the course of our proof, we will use the three quantities $\mu^{\max}(n)$, $B_k^{\up}(n)$ and $B_k^{\low}(n)$. We define $\mu^{\max}(n)$ by $$\mu^{\max}(n) := \max\{\frac{r_a}{d_a}\mid (d_1,r_1),\ldots(d_a,r_a)\text{ in }A_n\}.$$
