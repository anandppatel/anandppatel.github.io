2.3. Some lemmas on multiplication of polynomials

\begin{notation} Throughout Section 2.3, we will keep the following notation. \begin{enumerate} \item Suppose $n_{1}, \dots n_{k}$ are positive integers such that $\sum_{i=1}^{k}n_{i}=d$ and $\sum_{i=1}^{k}(n_{i}-1)=2r-2$ for some positive $r$.

\item Let $P_{m}$ denote the vector space of polynomials in one variable $z$ with degree $\leq m$.

\item Finally, let $\alpha_{1}, \dots, \alpha_{k} \in \A^{1} = \Spec k[z]$ be distinct points.

\end{enumerate}

\end{notation}

First Lemma

Lemma 2.22. (8F91)

There exists an $r-1$-dimensional subspace $V_{r-1} \subset P_{d-1}$ such that the multiplication-then-restriction map $$\begin{aligned}\rho: P_{1} \otimes V_{r-1} \to \bigoplus_{i=1}^{k}k[z]/(z-\alpha_{i})^{n_{i}-1}\end{aligned}$$ is an isomorphism.

Proof. (B891)

We will explicitly construct a vector space $V_{r-1}$ with the desired property.

Our construction is slightly different depending on the parities of the numbers $n_{i}$. So, for the convenience of the reader, let us first assume all $n_{i}$ are odd.

Then for each $i$, we define the polynomial $$\begin{aligned}p_{i} := \prod_{j \neq i}(z-\alpha_{j})^{n_{j}-1}.\end{aligned}$$

Next, for each $i$ consider the vector space $$\begin{aligned}A_{i} := \Span\left\{p_{i} \cdot 1,p_{i} \cdot (z-\alpha_{i})^{2}, p_{i}\cdot (z-\alpha_{i})^{4}, \dots,p_{i} \cdot (z-\alpha_{i})^{n_{i}-3} \right\}.\end{aligned}$$

Then it is immediately verified that $$\begin{aligned}\rho(P_{1} \otimes A_{i}) = (0, \dots, k[z]/(z-\alpha_{i})^{n_{i}-1}, 0 ,\dots).\end{aligned}$$ Note that $\dim A_{i} = (n_{i}-1)/2$. By letting $V_{r-1} = A_{1}+ \dots+ A_{k}$, the lemma follows in this case.

Now suppose $n_{1}$ is even. Since $\sum_{}^{}(n_{i}-1)$ is even, we may assume without loss of generality that $n_{2}$ is also even. Then we define the polynomials $$\begin{aligned}q_{1} = (z-\alpha_{2})^{n_{2}-2}\prod_{j \neq 1,2}(z-\alpha_{j})^{n_{j}-1}\\ q_{2} = (z-\alpha_{1})^{n_{1}-2}\prod_{j \neq 1,2}(z-\alpha_{j})^{n_{j}-1}.\end{aligned}$$ Next set $$\begin{aligned}B_{1,2}:= \Span\left\{ q_{1}, q_{1}(z-\alpha_{1})^{2}, q_{1}(z-\alpha_{1})^{4} \dots q_{1}(z-\alpha_{1})^{n_{1}-2} = q_{2}(z-\alpha_{2})^{n_{2}-2}, \dots, q_{2}(z-\alpha_{2})^{2}, q_{2} \right\}.\end{aligned}$$ Then it is straightforward to verify that $$\begin{aligned}\rho(P_{1} \otimes B_{1,2}) = (k[z]/(z-\alpha_{1})^{n_{1}-1},k[z]/(z-\alpha_{2})^{n_{2}-1},0,0, \dots ).\end{aligned}$$ Note that $\dim B_{1,2} = (n_{1}+ n_{2}-2)/2$. By pairing up the remaining even $n_{i}$ and creating the analogous vector spaces $B_{i,j}$, and then for odd $n_{i}$ using the spaces $A_{i}$, one can check that $$\begin{aligned}V_{r-1}:= \sum_{n_{i}\text{\,\,odd}} A_{i} + B_{1,2} + \dots\end{aligned}$$ has the desired property, i.e. that $\rho$ is an isomorphism.

Second Lemma

Lemma 2.23. (D46C)

Maintain the setup before the previous lemma. Then there exists an $r-1$-dimensional subspace $V_{r-1} \subset P_{d-1}$ such that the multiplication-then-restriction map $$\begin{aligned}\rho': P_{1}\otimes V_{r-1} \to k[z]/(z-\alpha_{1})^{n_{1}} \oplus_{i=2}^{k}k[z]/(z-\alpha_{i})^{n_{i}-1}\end{aligned}$$ has the following properties:

  1. $\rho'$ is injective.
  2. The image of $\rho'$ does not contain the element $$\begin{aligned}w = ((z-\alpha_{1})^{n_{1}-1},0,0,\dots).\end{aligned}$$
Proof. (B01F)

The spaces $V_{r-1}$ constructed in the proof of Lemma 2.22 [8F91] satisfy these two properties. We leave the verification to the reader.

Remark 2.24. (7AE4)

The conditions in these two lemmas are open – a general $V_{r-1} \subset P_{d-1}$ will have the desired properties in each lemma.

Theorem 2.25. (4CBD)

Let $\sum_{i=1}^{k}m_{k}=d$, and suppose $\sum_{i}^{}m_{i}-1 = 2r-2$. Suppose $\left( X,\ell \right)$ is a general point in $I_{k}\left( m_{1}, \dots , m_{k} \right)$. Then the point-line correspondence $\pi: \Sigma \to \G\left( 1,r \right)$ is mini-versal near the $k$-incident line $[\ell] \in \G\left( 1,r \right)$.

Proof. (847E)

The map $\rho$ in Lemma 2.22 [8F91] is the differential of (any of) the induced maps $$\begin{aligned}\left( U, \left[ \ell \right] \right) \to W_{m_{1}} \times \dots W_{m_{k}}\end{aligned}$$ from Proposition 2.7 [110D] (Here $U$ is an étale neighborhood of $\ell$). The theorem follows immediately from Proposition 2.7 [110D].

Corollary 2.26. (BC02)

Let $X \subset \P^{r}$ be a general degree $d \geq 2r-1$ hypersurface, and let $k = d-(2r-2)$. Then $$\begin{aligned}\left( \Sigma \subset \P \to \G\left( 1,r \right) \right)\end{aligned}$$ is versal at every $k$-incident line $\left[ \ell \right] \in \G\left( 1,r \right)$.

Proof. (0BAB)

When $k = d-2(r-1)$, the irreducible variety $I_{k}\left( m_{1}, \dots, m_{k} \right)$ maps generically finitely onto $\P^{N}$. The locus consisting of pairs $(X,\ell) \in I_{k}(m_{1}, \dots , m_{k})$ for which the point-line correspondence fails to be versal at $\ell$ is a strict (closed) subset of $I_{k}\left( m_{1}, \dots ,m_{k} \right)$ by Theorem 2.25 [4CBD]. Since this is true for all multiplicity data $\left( m_{1}, \dots, m_{k} \right)$, we conclude that there is a Zariski open subset of $\P^{N}$ for which the corollary holds.
