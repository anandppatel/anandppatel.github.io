3.2. In pursuit of a simple transposition

\begin{notation} Until otherwise specified, we adopt the following notation:

\begin{enumerate} \item $(X, \ell) \in I_{k-1}\left( m_{1}+m_{2}, m_{3}, \dots, m_{k} \right)$ is a general point, with $k = d-(2r-2)\ \geq 2$. Furthermore, we assume $m_{1} \neq m_{2}$.

\item We let $\A^{m_{1} + m_{2}- 1}$ denote the base of the mini-versal deformation $W_{m_{1}+m_{2}}$.

\item We let $Z_{m_{1},m_{2}} \subset \A^{m_{1}+m_{2}-1}$ be the locus parametrizing divisors of the form $m_{1}p + m_{2}q$.

\item $\pi : \Sigma \to \G\left( 1,r \right)$ denotes the point-line correspondence for $X$. \end{enumerate} \end{notation}

Lemma 3.4. (1FED)

$Z_{m_{1},m_{2}}$ is a uni-branched curve with a multiplicity two singularity at the origin $0 \in \A^{m_{1}+m_{2}-1}$.

Proof. (6290)

The family of deformations of $z^{m_{1}+m_{2}} = 0$ parametrized by the set $Z_{m_{1},m_{2}}$ is the image of the map $$\begin{aligned}t \mapsto (z-m_{2}t)^{m_{1}}(z+m_{1}t)^{m_{2}}.\end{aligned}$$ By expanding this polynomial in terms of $z$, we obtain an expression $$\begin{aligned}z^{m_{1}+m_{2}} + c_{2}t^{2}z^{m_{1}+m_{2}-2} + c_{3}t^{3}z^{m_{1}+m_{2}-3} + \dots + c_{m_{1}+m_{2}}t^{m_{1}+m_{2}}\end{aligned}$$ where, under the assumption that $m_{1} \neq m_{2}$, all coefficients $c_{j}$ are nonzero. The lemma follows.

Corollary 3.5. (2780)

Let $D \subset \A^{m_{1}+m_{2}-1}$ be a divisor given by the vanishing of an equation $f(a_{2}, \dots, a_{m_{1}+m_{2}})=0$, where $f$ is such that $0 \in D$, and such that $\frac{\partial}{\partial a_{2}}f(0) \neq 0$. Further suppose $D$ does not contain $Z_{m_{1},m_{2}}$.

Then $$\length (D \cap Z_{m_{1}, m_{2}}) = 2.$$

Proof. (DB23)

This easily follows from the parametrization of $Z_{m_{1},m_{2}}$ found in the proof of Lemma 3.4 [1FED].

Proposition 3.6. (8751)

Let $(X,\ell) \in I_{k-1}(m_{1}+m_{2}, m_{3}, \dots, m_{k})$ be a general point, and let $\pi: \Sigma \to \G\left( 1,r \right)$ be the point-line correspondence.

Denote by $$\nu: (U, [\ell]) \to (\A^{m_{1}+m_{2}-1} \times \A^{m_{3}-1} \times \dots \A^{m_{k}-1},0)$$ a local map to the mini-versal deformation space where $U \subset \G\left( 1,r \right)$ is a sufficiently small étale neighborhood of $\left[ \ell \right]$. Then

  1. The map $\nu$ is an immersion near $[\ell]$.
  2. The image $\nu(U)$ is transverse to $Z_{m_{1},m_{2}} \subset (\A^{m_{1}+m_{2}-1}, 0, \dots, 0)$ in the sense of Corollary 3.5 [2780].

Proof. (4A46)

The two statements are proved at the level of tangent spaces. They follow, respectively, by applying points $1$ and $2$ in Lemma 2.23 [D46C].

``No unintended consequences'' lemma

Now suppose $$\begin{aligned}(X,\ell,\ell') \in I_{k-1,k}(m_{1}+m_{2}, m_{3}, \dots, m_{k} \mid m_{1}, \dots, m_{k})\end{aligned}$$ is a general point. The next lemma and corollary show that the condition of possessing the $k-1$-incident line $\ell$ has no effect on the $k$-incident line $\ell'$ of $X$. Hence, there are in general ``no unintended consequences elsewhere'' for having a $k-1$-incident line.

Lemma 3.7. (3BF7)

The point-line correspondence $$\begin{aligned}\pi : \Sigma \to \G\left( 1,r \right)\end{aligned}$$ is mini-versal near $[\ell'] \in \G\left( 1,r \right)$.

Proof. (B0D3)

After using the $\PGL_{r+1}$-action, we may assume $$\begin{aligned}\ell = [x_{0}:x_{1}]\\ \ell' = [x_{2}:x_{3}]\end{aligned}$$ and therefore, that the homogeneous ideal of the union $\ell \cup \ell'$ is $$\begin{aligned}I_{\ell \cup \ell'} = (x_{0}x_{2}, x_{1}x_{2}, x_{0}x_{3}, x_{1}x_{3}, x_{4}, x_{5}, \dots x_{r}).\end{aligned}$$

Therefore, we may write the defining equation of $X$ in the form $$\begin{aligned}F = p(x_{0},x_{1}) + q(x_{2},x_{3}) + \sum_{i=0,1 ; j=2,3}^{}x_{i}x_{j}A_{i,j} + \sum_{i=4}^{r}x_{i}G_{i}\end{aligned}$$ where $A_{i,j} \in \Gamma (O_{\P^{r}}(d-2))$, and $G_{i} \in \Gamma (O_{\P^{r}}(d-1))$.

We let $a_{i,j} = A_{i,j}|_{\ell'}$, and $g_{i} = G_{i}|_{\ell'}$.

As in the proof of Lemma 2.20 [78AE], we apply the infinitesimal substitutions $$\begin{aligned}x_{0}& \mapsto x_{0}+\varepsilon \ell_{0}\\ x_{1}& \mapsto x_{1} + \varepsilon \ell_{1}\\ x_{2}& \mapsto x_{2}\\ x_{3}& \mapsto x_{3}\\ x_{4}& \mapsto x_{4} + \varepsilon \ell_{4}\\ \vdots& \\ x_{r} &\mapsto x_{r} + \varepsilon \ell_{r}\end{aligned}$$ and then mod out by the ideal $I_{\ell'} = (x_{0}, x_{1}, x_{4}, \dots, x_{r})$. (Here $\ell_{i}$ are linear forms in the variables/$x_{2}$ and $x_{3}$.)

The result is the infinitesimal family $$\begin{aligned}f_{\varepsilon} = q(x_{2},x_{3}) + \varepsilon \ell_{0}(x_{2}a_{0,2} + x_{3}a_{0,3}) + \varepsilon \ell_{1}(x_{2}a_{1,2} + x_{3}a_{1,3}) + \sum_{i = 4}^{r}\varepsilon \ell_{i} g_{i}.\end{aligned}$$

Set $g_{0} := x_{2}a_{0,2}+x_{3}a_{0,3}$ and $g_{1} := x_{2}a_{1,2} + x_{3}a_{1,3}$. Then, general choices of the four polynomials $a_{i,j}$ yield general choices of $g_{0}$ and $g_{1}$.

These observations tell us that the first-order analysis of $\pi: \Sigma \to \G\left( 1,r \right)$ near $[\ell']$ is exactly the same as in the case for a general point $(X,\ell') \in I_{k}(m_{1}, \dots m_{k})$. In particular, since $g_{0}, g_{1}, g_{4}, \dots, g_{r}$ are taken to be generic, $\pi$ is mini-versal near $[\ell']$.

Corollary 3.8. (F116)

Let $(X,\ell) \in I_{k-1}(m_{1}+m_{2}, m_{3}, \dots, m_{k})$ be a general point. Then the point-line correspondence $$\begin{aligned}\pi : \Sigma \to \G\left( 1,r \right)\end{aligned}$$ is mini-versal at all $k$-incident lines $[\ell'] \in \G\left( 1,r \right)$.

Proof. (4274)

Indeed, the variety $I''_{k-1, k}(m_{1}+m_{2}, m_{3}, \dots, m_{k} \mid m_{1}, m_{2}, \dots, m_{k})$ is irreducible, and generically finite over $I_{k-1}(m_{1}+m_{2}, m_{3}, \dots, m_{k})$. The previous lemma shows that there is a Zariski open subset of $I_{k-1,k}$ with $\ell'$ versal. The corollary follows.

Lemma 3.9. (D56F)

Let $(X,\ell) \in I_{k-1}(n_{1}, \dots, n_{k-1})$ be a general point. Then $X$ does not possess any other $k-1$-incident lines.

Proof. (FD4B)

This follows from a simple dimension count – we leave it to the reader.

Theorem 3.10. (089B)

The monodromy group $G$ contains a simple transposition.

Proof. (8535)

Let $(X, \ell) \in I_{k-1}(m_{1}+m_{2}, \dots, m_{k})$ be a general point.

All $k$-incident lines of multiplicity $(m_{1}, \dots, m_{k})$ are simple, i.e. occur with multiplicity one, according to Corollary 3.8 [F116]. The line $[\ell]$ occurs as a multiplicity $2$ $k$-incident line of type $(m_{1}, \dots, m_{k})$, according to Corollary 3.5 [2780].

Therefore, above the point $[X] \in \P^{N}$, the correspondence $I_{k}(m_{1}, \dots, m_{k})$ has exactly one nonreduced point of length $2$, and all other points are reduced.

Since the curve $Z_{m_{1},m_{2}} \subset \A^{m_{1}+m_{2}-1}$ is irreducible, the two nearby $k$-incident lines (for a nearby hypersurface $X'$) limiting to $[\ell]$ experience monodromy.

Hence we obtain a simple transposition in the monodromy group $G$.

Moduli of lines meeting $X$ infrequently

Definition 3.11. (19A2)

Let $k \geq 4$. We define $i_{(m_{1}, \dots, m_{k})}: I_{k}(m_{1}, \dots , m_{k}) \dashrightarrow \m_{0,k}/S_{k}$ to be the map sending $(X,\ell)$ to $\left[ \ell \cap_{set} X \subset \ell \right]$. Here , subscript ``set'' means ``set theoretic''.

Corollary 3.12. (B486)

Assume $k = d- (2r-2) \geq 4$, and let $X$ be a general degree $d$ hypersurface. Then any two distinct $(X,\ell), (X,\ell') \in I_{k}(m_{1}, \dots, m_{k})$ have distinct images under $i_{(m_{1},\dots,m_{k})}$.

Proof. (F367)

Let $J \subset I_{k}(m_{1}, \dots ,m_{k}) \times_{\P^{N}}I_{k}(m_{1}, \dots ,m_{k}) $ denote the closure of the locus of pairs $\left( (X,\ell), (X,\ell') \right)$ having equal images under $i_{(m_{1},\dots,m_{k})}$, and such that $\ell \cap \ell'$ is empty.

By Lemma 3.2 [31C3], The fiber product $I_{k}(m_{1}, \dots ,m_{k}) \times_{\P^{N}}I_{k}(m_{1}, \dots ,m_{k}) \setminus \Delta $ is irreducible, where $\Delta$ denotes the diagonal.

It is easy to exhibit two lines $\ell, \ell' \subset \P^{r}$ and a degree $d$ hypersurface $X$ such that $\ell$ and $\ell'$ are $k$-incident with multiplicity $(m_{1}, \dots, m_{k})$ and such that $i_{(m_{1}, \dots, m_{k})}(\ell) \neq i_{(m_{1}, \dots, m_{k})}(\ell')$.

Therefore, $J$, being a closed set, must have dimension strictly smaller than this fiber product's dimension. But the latter is the dimension of $\P^{N}$, i.e. the locus $J$ projects to a strict subset of $\P^{N}$. This proves the corollary.
