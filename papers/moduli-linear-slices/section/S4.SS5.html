4.5. Straight trees and the proof of Theorem 1.3 [5763]

\begin{notation} During this section, we assume $d = 2r+1$ and $X \subset \P^{r}$ a general hypersurface of degree $d$. \end{notation}

Definition 4.19. (52F9)

A straight tree is any stable curve $(P,s_{1}, \dots, s_{d})$ having dual graph of the form \tikzset{main node/.style={thick,circle,draw},}

\begin{tikzpicture} \node[main node, label={\small $2$}] (1) {}; \node[main node, label={\small $1$}] (2) [right = .7cm of 1] {}; \node[main node, label={\small $1$}] (3) [right = .7cm of 2] {};

\node[main node, label={\small $1$}] (4) [right = .7cm of 3] {}; \node[main node, label={\small $2$}] (5) [right = .7cm of 4] {};

\path (1) edge (2); \path (2) edge (3); \path (3) – node[auto=false]{\ldots} (4); \path (4) edge (5); \end{tikzpicture}

(There are a total of $d-2$ vertices.)

Lemma 4.20. (0E74)

Assume $d \geq 4$. There are $d!/8$ distinct straight trees.

Proof. (6DD2)

Indeed, we may exchange the two points on each extremal component of a straght tree, or we may flip the entire tree over, without altering the moduli of the straight tree. Therefore, we must divide $d!$ by $2 \times 2 \times 2$.

Lemma 4.21. (FD94)

Suppose $z \in Z$ is such that $\alpha(z) \in \barm_{0,d}$ is a straight tree. Then $\hat{[\ell]} := \beta(z) \in \Sigma^{[d]}$ lies over a $3$-incident line $\ell$. Furthermore, the multiplicity of $\ell$ is $(1,a,b)$, where $a+b = d-1$.

Proof. (B843)

$X$ possesses no $2$-incident lines. The lemma follows from Proposition 4.14 [B98F], and from the observation in Remark 4.12 [CE30].

Lemma 4.22. (D611)

Let $a,b$ be positive integers such that $a+b=d-1$. Then for every $3$-incident line $\ell$ of multiplicity $(1,a,b)$, and every point $\hat{[\ell]} \in \Sigma^{[d]}$ lying over $\ell$, there are precisely $a!b!/4$ points $z \in \beta^{-1}(\hat{[\ell]})$ such that $\alpha(z)$ is a straight tree.

Proof. (BA25)

Let $p, q$ be the points of multiplicity $a$ and $b$ on $\ell$. Then the variety $\beta^{-1}(\hat{[\ell]})$ is the collection of stable curves obtained by attaching all rational tails with $a$ marked points to the point $p$, and similarly with $q$. The lemma follows, after recalling that the two extremal points in a straight tree may be exchanged without changing the moduli of the tree – this explains the division by $4$.

Lemma 4.23. (28EA)

For every $3$-incident line $\ell$ of multiplicity $(1,1,d-2)$, and every point $\hat{[\ell]} \in \Sigma^{[d]}$ lying over $\ell$, there are precisely $(d-2)!/2$ points $z \in \beta^{-1}(\hat{[\ell]})$ such that $\alpha(z)$ is a straight tree.

Proof. (B7CA)

The proof is completely analogous to the proof of the previous lemma. The only difference is that we are only attaching rational tails to one point on $\ell$, hence we must divide by two only once.

Theorem 4.24. (73C2)

For any multiplicity $(a,b,c)$, let $n_{a,b,c}$ denote the number of $3$-incident lines to $X$ having the given multiplicity. Then the degree of the map $\alpha: Z \to \barm_{0,d}$ is $$\begin{aligned}\deg \alpha = 2 \sum_{a \geq b > 1}^{}n_{(a,b,1)} + 4n_{d-2,1,1}.\end{aligned}$$

Furthermore, $\deg \alpha = \deg \mu_{1}$.

Proof. (F22F)

The last statement is clear.

By symmetry considerations, every straight tree $[P] \in \barm_{0,d}$ has the same number of preimages in $Z$, all preimages arise from $3$-incident lines, by Lemma 4.21 [FD94], and all preimages are unramified points of $\alpha$, by Corollary 4.9 [4F92]. Above a $3$-incident line $\ell$ with multiplicity $(a,b,1)$, where $a,b>1$, there are $\frac{d!}{a!b!}$ points in $s^{-1}([\ell])$, by Lemma 4.4 [ADE2]. Each contributes $a!b!/4$ straight trees by Lemma 4.22 [D611]. Therefore, we obtain $\frac{d!}{a!b!}\frac{a!b!}{4} = \frac{d!}{4}$ total straight trees arising from $3$-incident lines with multiplicities $(a,b,1)$, where $a,b > 1$. Since there are $\frac{d!}{8}$ straight trees total (Lemma 4.20 [0E74]), we see that each such line contributes $2$ to the degree of $\alpha$. The same argument, using Lemma 4.23 [28EA] explains the coefficient $4$ for lines of type $(d-2,1,1)$.
