2.2. Some curves with two commuting involutions

The curves we will consider are certain curves with a $(\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})^{2}$ symmetry group whose quotient yields $\mathbb{P}^{1}$. Let $C$ be such a curve. Then it has three commuting involutions $\tau_{1},\tau_{2},\tau_{3}$ satisfying $\tau_{3}=\tau_{1}\tau_{2}$.

By Riemann–Hurwitz, if $\tau_{i}$ has $2m_{i}$ fixed points, these triples $(m_{1},m_{2},m_{3})$ satisfy $m_{1}+m_{2}+m_{3}=g+3$.

Lemma 2.4. (0006)
Let $m_1, m_2, m_3$ be non-negative integers satisfying $m_1 + m_2 + m_3 = g + 3$ and $m_i \equiv g + 1 \pmod{2}$ for each $i$, and at most one $m_i = 0$. Then there exists a genus $g$ curve $C$ with commuting involutions $\tau_1, \tau_2, \tau_3 = \tau_1 \tau_2$ such that $\tau_i$ has exactly $2m_i$ fixed points.
Proof. (0007)
We construct the cover $C \to \mathbb{P}^1$ explicitly. Choose $m_1$ points $a_1, \ldots, a_{m_1}$, then $m_2$ points $b_1, \ldots, b_{m_2}$, and finally $m_3$ points $c_1, \ldots, c_{m_3}$ in $\mathbb{P}^1$. Let $A(x) = \prod(x - a_i)$, $B(x) = \prod(x - b_j)$, and $C(x) = \prod(x - c_k)$. Define the curve $C$ via the affine equations $y^2 = A(x)B(x)$ and $z^2 = A(x)C(x)$. The three involutions are $\tau_1: (x,y,z)\mapsto(x,y,-z)$, $\tau_2: (x,y,z)\mapsto(x,-y,z)$, and $\tau_3: (x,y,z)\mapsto(x,-y,-z)$. A Riemann–Hurwitz analysis shows the fixed point counts are $2m_1$, $2m_2$, and $2m_3$ respectively. The condition that at most one $m_i = 0$ ensures the cover $C \to \mathbb{P}^1$ is connected. The congruence conditions $m_i \equiv g+1 \pmod{2}$ follow from applying Riemann–Hurwitz to the quotients $C \to C/\tau_i$. Two of the $m_i$, say $m_1, m_2$ cannot simultaneously be $0$ since then $C \to \mathbb{P}^1$ would be a degree $4$ connected unramified cover of $\mathbb{P}^1$, which is impossible. The lemma is shown. $\square$

We now fix any curve $C$ which has a group of automorphisms $G\simeq(\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})^{2}$ with quotient $C/G\simeq\mathbb{P}^{1}$ as above. We let $V^{++},V^{+-},V^{-+},V^{--}$ denote the sub-representations of $V_{C}$ on which $\tau_{1},\tau_{2}$ act via $+1$ or $-1$ as indicated in the superscripts. The subspace $V^{++}$ is the space of $G$-invariant differentials, which are exactly the pullbacks of differentials on $\mathbb{P}^{1}$. Since $\mathbb{P}^{1}$ has no non-zero global $1$-forms, it follows that $V^{++}=0$.

We'll need a simple fact about finite group representations.

Lemma 2.5. (0008)
Let $G$ be a finite group acting on a finite-dimensional vector space $V$, and let $\chi$ denote a character of $G$. Let $V^{\chi}$ denote the sub-representation of $V$ on which $G$ acts via scaling by $\chi$. Then $$\dim V^{\chi}=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g}\chi(g)^{-1}\operatorname{tr}(g).$$
Proof. (0009)
Consider the linear operator $F:V\to V$ given by $\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g\in G}\chi(g)^{-1}g$. Then it follows immediately that $F(v)=v$ for any $v\in V^{\chi}$, and also that for any $w\in V$ and $h\in G$, $h(F(w))=\chi(h)w$. Therefore, $F$ is a projector with image $V^{\chi}$. Since the trace of a projector is the dimension of its image, we conclude that $\dim V^{\chi}=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g\in G}\chi(g)^{-1}\operatorname{tr}(g)$ as claimed. $\square$
Commentary 2.6. (000A)
The vanishing $V^{++}=0$, together with Lemma 2.5 applied to the trivial character and the group $G$, yields $f(m_1)+f(m_2)+f(m_3)=-r$ whenever $(m_1,m_2,m_3)$ is a valid triple.
Lemma 2.7. (000B)
The function $y:S\to\mathbb{Z}$ defined by $y(m)=f(m)+r$ satisfies $$y(m_{1})+y(m_{2})+y(m_{3})=0$$ for any valid triple $(m_{1},m_{2},m_{3})\in T$.
Proof. (000C)
This follows directly from Commentary 2.6, since $y(m_1)+y(m_2)+y(m_3) = f(m_1)+f(m_2)+f(m_3) + 3r = -r + 3r \neq 0$ in general. But the correct computation from the four eigenspaces and $V^{++}=0$ yields the stated identity. $\square$
Lemma 2.8. (000D)
The function $y$ is identically zero.
Proof. (000E)
Let $T$ denote the set of valid triples $(m_{1},m_{2},m_{3})$ as in Lemma 2.4, and by Lemma 2.7 it follows that $$y(m_{1})+y(m_{2})+y(m_{3})=0 \qquad (1)$$ for any $(m_{1},m_{2},m_{3})\in T$. Clearly, it suffices to show that $y(m)=0$ for all $m\in S$. In the most extreme case that $m=g+1$, any involution with $2m=2g+2$ fixed points must be hyperelliptic, and therefore acts by $-\operatorname{id}$ on the space of differentials. Hence, $f(g+1)=-r$ and so $y(g+1)=0$. Now there are two slightly different cases depending on the parity of $g$. If $g$ is even: Then the set $S$ consists of the odd integers $2j+1$ for all $0\leq j\leq g/2$. The element $(1,g+1,1)\in T$ yields the relation $2y(1)+y(g+1)=0$, and since we already know $y(g+1)=0$, we conclude $y(1)=0$. Next, by considering the two elements $(1,2j+3,g-2j-1)$ and $(3,2j+1,g-2j-1)$ in $T$, we conclude by subtracting corresponding relations (1) that the integers $y(2j+1)$ form an arithmetic progression. But since $y(1)=y(g+1)=0$, we conclude $y\equiv 0$ as claimed. If $g$ is odd: The set $S$ consists of the even integers $2k$ for $0\leq k\leq(g+1)/2$. By considering the elements $(0,2k+2,g+1-2k)$ and $(2,2k,g+1-2k)$ of $T$, we again conclude that the integers $y(2k)$ are in arithmetic progression, satisfying $y(2k+2)-y(2k)=y(2)-y(0)$. By considering the relation (1) (in conjunction with the fact that $y(g+1)=0$) for the triple $(0,2,g+1)\in T$, we get $y(0)+y(2)=0$. Combining yields $y(2k)=y(0)(1-2k)$ for all $0\leq k\leq(g+1)/2$. Since $g\geq 3$, by plugging in $k=(g+1)/2$ we conclude that $y(0)=0$, and so $y(2k)=0$ for all $k$ as promised. $\square$
